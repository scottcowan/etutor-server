# Bread Baking Science — Experiment Guide

**Curriculum topic:** `exp_bread_baking_science`
**Age:** Y3–9 (ages 8–14)
**Equipment needed:** Strong white bread flour (300g), dried yeast (one 7g sachet), salt (1 tsp), warm water (~200ml), a mixing bowl, a baking tray, an oven (adult supervision required), a sharp knife. Optionally: scales for comparing loaf weight before and after baking.

---

## What the Child Is Testing

This experiment works best as a series of observations across the baking process. There are five distinct chemistry moments:

| Stage | What to observe | What chemistry is happening |
|---|---|---|
| **Mixing and kneading** | Dough becomes stretchy and elastic as you knead | Gluten network forming — protein strands linking up |
| **First rise (proving)** | Dough roughly doubles in size over 1–2 hours | Yeast fermenting sugars → CO2 + ethanol; CO2 trapped in gluten network |
| **Punching down** | Dough deflates immediately when pressed | CO2 escapes; the gluten network is intact but the gas is gone |
| **Baking — oven spring** | Dough rises rapidly in first 10 min of baking | Residual yeast frantic activity before dying at ~60°C; trapped gases expand |
| **Crust browning** | Surface turns golden-brown and smells amazing | Maillard reaction — amino acids + sugars react at high temperature |

## Tutor Script — Predict, Observe, Explain

### Before mixing

Ask one predictive question before starting:

- "Bread has been made for at least 14,000 years — before writing, before metal tools, before cities. How do you think early humans discovered that flour + water + time + heat = bread?"
- "What is making bread rise? I'll give you a clue — it's alive. What do you think it does, and why does it make gas?"
- "If we left the bread dough on the counter for a week without baking it, what do you think would happen?"

### Kneading stage

Mix flour, yeast, salt, and warm water. Knead for 8–10 minutes. The child can do most of the kneading. Observe the dough changing from shaggy and sticky to smooth and elastic.

1. "What happened to the dough as you kneaded? It changed texture — why do you think physical working changes it?"
2. "Pull a small piece of kneaded dough and stretch it slowly. Hold it up to the light. What can you see?" (With well-developed gluten, you can stretch a thin translucent membrane — the "windowpane test.")
3. "What would happen if we didn't knead it — would the bread still rise? Would it hold its shape?"

### First rise — proving

Cover the bowl with a damp cloth and leave in a warm place. Check at 30 minutes and at 60 minutes.

1. "Before we check — what do you predict has happened? What is the yeast doing right now?"
2. When doubled: "What is filling up the dough? Where is that gas coming from?"
3. "Why does the dough need to be warm — what happens if you put it in the fridge? Does the yeast stop?" (No — it just slows dramatically. Cold-proofed dough in the fridge overnight is a real technique that develops more flavour.)
4. "Can you smell anything? What does rising dough smell like?" (A faint yeasty, slightly alcoholic smell — the ethanol produced alongside CO2.)

### Punch down

Gently press the dough with your fist. It collapses dramatically.

1. "What happened? Where did all the rise go?"
2. "The gluten network is still there — what escaped?"
3. "We're going to let it rise again before baking. Why bother — why not just bake it now after the first rise?"

### Baking

Shape the dough, let it prove a second time (30 minutes), then bake at 200°C for 25–30 minutes.

Before putting in the oven: "In the first few minutes in the oven, the dough will rise rapidly — more than during proving. Why do you think the heat causes a sudden burst of rising?"

After baking: tap the bottom of the loaf. A hollow sound means fully baked (the structure is set and dry inside).

1. "What colour is the crust? Why does it go brown when the inside stays white/cream?" 
2. "The crust smells different from plain flour — why does heat create that smell and colour?"
3. (If you have scales) "Weigh the loaf and compare to the raw dough weight. What happened to the mass? Where did it go?"

### The chemistry connections

**Gluten formation:** Wheat flour contains two proteins, glutenin and gliadin. When mixed with water and mechanically worked, these proteins unfold, link together, and form long elastic strands called gluten. Gluten is what makes bread dough stretchy and allows it to trap gas bubbles. Rice flour, corn flour, and potato flour contain no gluten — which is why gluten-free breads are denser and crumblier. Kneading accelerates and strengthens the gluten network.

**Yeast fermentation:** Yeast (Saccharomyces cerevisiae) is a single-celled fungus. In the warm, sugary dough environment, yeast digests simple sugars (from the flour's starch partially breaking down) and produces CO2 and ethanol through anaerobic fermentation:
- C₆H₁₂O₆ → 2CO2 + 2CH₃CH₂OH (glucose → carbon dioxide + ethanol)
- The CO2 is trapped in the gluten network → bread rises
- The ethanol evaporates in the oven (this is why baked bread doesn't make you drunk)
- The yeast dies at ~60°C — the structure is "set" by then

**Oven spring:** In the first few minutes at oven temperature, the yeast has a final burst of activity before dying. The CO2 and other gases expand rapidly with heat (hot gases expand — Charles's law). The gluten proteins also stiffen as heat denatures them, fixing the structure in place.

**Maillard reaction:** At around 140°C, amino acids and reducing sugars on the bread surface react in a complex cascade of chemical reactions. This is the Maillard reaction (named after French chemist Louis-Camille Maillard, 1912). It produces hundreds of flavour compounds and the characteristic golden-brown colour. It's not burning — the loaf's interior, which never exceeds 100°C (water keeps it below boiling point), stays pale. The Maillard reaction is also responsible for the flavour of roasted coffee, seared steak, caramelised onions, and biscuits.

**Why salt?** Salt (sodium chloride) does several things: it strengthens the gluten network by affecting protein interactions, it controls yeast activity (too much salt kills yeast), and it flavours the bread. Try making bread without salt — it produces a bland, slightly slimy loaf.

**Connection to yeast_bread_rise and fermentation_yeast:** The yeast_bread_rise experiment focuses on yeast activity and CO2 production. This experiment extends that to the full system: the gluten network that traps the CO2, the physical and chemical changes during baking, and the Maillard browning that makes the crust. They're the same organism doing the same chemistry — this experiment shows what happens next.

### Bloom-level questions by age

| Age | Question | Bloom level |
|---|---|---|
| 8–9 | "What are the three main things yeast produces in the dough?" (CO2, ethanol, heat) | 1–2 — Remember/Understand |
| 10–11 | "If you add too much salt to the dough, the bread doesn't rise well. Why not?" | 3 — Apply |
| 11–12 | "The interior of a baked loaf never goes above 100°C, but the crust reaches 150–180°C. What does this tell you about the role of water inside the bread?" | 4 — Analyse |
| 12–13 | "Sourdough bread uses wild yeast and bacteria instead of commercial dried yeast. The bacteria produce lactic and acetic acids — the same as in yoghurt and vinegar. What effect do these acids have on the flavour? On the texture?" | 4–5 — Analyse/Evaluate |
| 13+ | "Design an experiment to test whether kneading time affects the final loaf volume. What would you control, what would you vary, and how would you measure the outcome fairly?" | 6 — Create |

## The Bigger Picture Questions

After the practical, connect outward:

- **To fermentation_yeast and enzyme_activity:** "The amylase enzymes in flour break starch into simple sugars during proving — this feeds the yeast. Saliva also contains amylase. What does that mean for how your body starts digesting bread before it even reaches your stomach?"
- **To history:** "Leavened bread (bread that rises) appears in ancient Egypt around 3000 BC — almost certainly discovered by accident when wild yeasts from the air landed in a flour-water mixture left out. Beer fermentation was discovered at the same time, by the same yeast. What do bread and beer have in common chemically?"
- **To food science:** "Biscuits, cakes, and muffins use baking powder instead of yeast. Baking powder is a chemical that releases CO2 when it gets wet and hot, without needing a living organism. What are the advantages of baking powder over yeast? What would you lose?"
- **To ecology:** "Commercial yeast was isolated and mass-produced starting in the 19th century. Before that, bakers kept 'starter' — live yeast culture maintained for decades or centuries. Some San Francisco sourdough starters are over 150 years old. What is being preserved in a sourdough starter that supermarket yeast can't provide?"
- **To materials science:** "The gluten in bread creates an elastic network. Chefs use similar protein networks in other foods — the elasticity of mozzarella, the stretch of taffy. What other food structures depend on protein networks?"
