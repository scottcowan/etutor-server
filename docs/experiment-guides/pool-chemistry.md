# Pool Chemistry — Experiment Guide

**Curriculum topic:** `exp_pool_chemistry`
**Age:** Y6–9 (ages 11–14)
**Equipment needed:** Pool test kit (strips or liquid reagents), pool water sample

---

## What the Child Is Testing

A pool test kit measures four things. Each has a target range and an explanation:

| Parameter | Target range | What it means |
|---|---|---|
| **pH** | 7.2–7.6 | How acidic or alkaline the water is. Below 7.2 = too acidic (irritates eyes, corrodes equipment). Above 7.8 = too alkaline (chlorine becomes ineffective, water goes cloudy) |
| **Free chlorine** | 1–3 ppm | How much active chlorine is available to kill bacteria. Below 1 = not sanitising. Above 5 = irritating to skin and eyes |
| **Total alkalinity** | 80–120 ppm | Acts as a buffer that stops pH swinging wildly. Low = pH unstable. High = hard to adjust pH |
| **Cyanuric acid (stabiliser)** | 30–50 ppm | Protects chlorine from UV sunlight degradation. Too high = "chlorine lock" (chlorine present but inactive) |
| **Calcium hardness** | 200–400 ppm | Too low = water eats the plaster/metal. Too high = calcium deposits (scale) on surfaces |

## Tutor Script — Predict, Observe, Explain

### Before testing

Ask one predictive question before the child reads the results:

- "Before you dip the strip — what do you think the pH will be? Lower than 7, exactly 7, or higher?"
- "The water looks a little green today. What do you think that might mean about the chlorine level?"
- "Why do you think we test the pool chemistry rather than just adding chemicals whenever we feel like it?"

### Reading the results

Have the child read each value aloud and compare to the target range.

For each one that's out of range:
1. "Is that too high or too low?"
2. "What do you think that means is happening in the water right now?"
3. "What would you add to fix it?" (let them guess — correct answers vary by parameter)

### The chemistry connections

**pH:** This is the same pH scale from the red cabbage experiment. A pool is slightly alkaline — why? Because human skin sits at about pH 5.5, and water close to neutral is less irritating than strongly acidic water.

**Chlorine:** Chlorine dissolved in water forms hypochlorous acid (HOCl) — a weak acid that punches through cell membranes of bacteria and viruses. The same chemistry that bleaches clothes destroys pathogens. Why does the sun destroy it? UV breaks the Cl–O bond.

**Cyanuric acid:** Think of it as sunscreen for chlorine. It bonds loosely to chlorine molecules and releases them slowly, protecting them from UV. Too much = too strong a grip = chlorine locked up and unavailable. This is "chlorine lock" — the test shows chlorine present but the water isn't sanitised.

**Alkalinity vs pH:** These seem similar but aren't. Alkalinity is how much the water resists pH change (buffering capacity). Low alkalinity means pH jumps wildly with small additions. Test alkalinity first, fix it first — then fix pH.

**Calcium hardness:** Water without enough calcium dissolves the pool walls. Water with too much deposits calcium carbonate (the same limescale in kettles) on surfaces and in pipe lines.

### Bloom-level questions by age

| Age | Question | Bloom level |
|---|---|---|
| 11–12 | "Why do we test pH first before adding chlorine?" | 3 — Apply |
| 12–13 | "The chlorine reads 2 ppm but the water is still a bit green. What else could be causing that?" | 4 — Analyse |
| 13–14 | "If cyanuric acid is too high, you can't fix it by adding chemicals — you have to partially drain and refill. Why?" | 4–5 — Analyse/Evaluate |
| 14+ | "Design a monitoring system for a public swimming pool that would alert a lifeguard when any parameter goes out of range. What sensors would you need?" | 6 — Create |

## The Bigger Picture Questions

After the practical test, connect outward:

- **To drinking water:** "Your tap water also has pH, hardness, and chlorine measured. How is that different from pool chemistry?"
- **To the environment:** "Where does the water go when you drain part of the pool? Does it matter that it has chlorine in it?"
- **To industrial water treatment:** "A town's drinking water treatment plant does the same job as pool chemistry but for millions of litres. How would they test all of it?"
- **To history:** "Before chlorination (introduced in the early 1900s), outdoor public pools were a major source of typhoid and cholera outbreaks. How did adding chlorine change that?"
