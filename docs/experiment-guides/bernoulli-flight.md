# Bernoulli Effect and Flight — Experiment Guide

**Curriculum topic:** `exp_bernoulli_flight`
**Age:** Y3–8 (ages 8–13)
**Equipment needed:** A sheet of A4 paper, a ping pong ball (or a light balloon), a hairdryer or hand-held fan, a ruler, a drinking straw, two empty plastic bottles (same size), tape. Optionally: a piece of card for making a simple paper aeroplane wing cross-section.

---

## What the Child Is Testing

| Demonstration | What you observe | What it reveals |
|---|---|---|
| **Paper lift** | Hold a strip of paper below your bottom lip and blow over the top surface. It rises toward the airstream. | Moving air has lower pressure. The still air below pushes the paper up. |
| **Ping pong ball in jet stream** | Blow a ping pong ball from a straw or position it in a hairdryer stream. The ball stays trapped in the airstream. | Moving air creates a low-pressure "tube" — still air on the outside pushes the ball in. |
| **Two hanging bottles** | Hang two empty plastic bottles close together and blow between them. They swing toward each other. | Again: fast air between them → low pressure → outside (still) air pressure pushes them together. |
| **Paper aeroplane wing** | Hold a cross-section wing shape and blow across it. Measure or observe the lift generated. | An asymmetric aerofoil shape generates lift because air travels faster over the curved top surface. |

## Tutor Script — Predict, Observe, Explain

### Before starting

Ask one predictive question before any demonstration:

- "If I blow over the top of a piece of paper, what do you think will happen — will the paper go up toward the airflow, or away from it? What's your instinct?"
- "Aeroplanes weigh hundreds of tonnes. What do you think is holding them up in the air?"
- "Have you ever been in a fast-moving car with the window open and felt your arm get pulled outward? Do you know why?"

### Demonstration 1 — Paper lift

Tear a strip of paper about 3 cm wide, 20 cm long. Hold one end just below your bottom lip so it hangs down and curves slightly. Blow steadily across the top surface.

1. "What happened? Did that match your prediction?"
2. "If blowing over the top caused it to rise — what does that tell you about the air pressure on top vs underneath?"
3. "Where is the pressure greater — above the paper where you're blowing, or below it where the air is still?"

### Demonstration 2 — Ping pong ball in airstream

Use a hairdryer on cool setting (or blow through a straw). Position a ping pong ball in the airstream at about 30° from horizontal. The ball should hover and stay trapped in the stream. Tilt the dryer and the ball follows.

1. "Why doesn't the ball fall — it has no support holding it up?"
2. "When you tilt the dryer sideways, the ball stays in the stream. Why doesn't it fly outward?"
3. "The fast-moving air creates a low-pressure zone in the stream. Still air pushes inward from outside. How does that explain the ball staying trapped?"

### Demonstration 3 — Two bottles attract

Hang two identical plastic bottles on strings so they hang about 5 cm apart. Blow steadily between them. They should swing toward each other (not apart).

1. "Before you try: if you blow between two objects, do you expect them to move apart (blown apart) or toward each other?"
2. Observe them moving together. "Does this match your intuition? Most people predict the wrong direction. Why do you think our intuition is wrong here?"
3. "What is pushing the bottles toward each other — is it the moving air between them, or the still air on the outside?"

### The physics connections

**Bernoulli's principle:** Daniel Bernoulli (1700–1782) described a fundamental relationship in fluid dynamics: in a moving fluid (air or water), faster-moving fluid has lower pressure than slower-moving fluid. This seems counterintuitive — we associate "fast and strong" with "high pressure." But pressure in a fluid is the sideways force the fluid exerts, not its forward momentum. When a fluid speeds up (as in a narrow tube or over a curved surface), its energy shifts from pressure energy to kinetic energy — so pressure drops.

**Correct vs simplified explanation of flight:** The "standard" school explanation — "air over the top of the wing must travel faster because it has further to go, so it has lower pressure" — is partially wrong. Wings don't require air parcels to "reunite" at the back edge. The real reason the top-surface air moves faster is that the wing is angled (angle of attack) and shaped asymmetrically — the airflow is deflected downward, and by Newton's third law, the wing is pushed up. Bernoulli's principle explains the pressure difference once the velocity difference exists; Newton explains why the velocity difference exists. Both are correct and both are needed for a complete explanation.

**Angle of attack:** Most of a wing's lift at low speed comes from angle of attack (the wing tilting into the oncoming air, deflecting it downward), not aerofoil shape. You can demonstrate this: a flat piece of card at the right angle of attack generates lift. This is why paper aeroplanes fly despite having no curved aerofoil cross-section.

**Why birds can glide:** A bird spreads its wings and glides without flapping. The wing shape and angle deflect air downward, generating lift. Gravity is pulling the bird down, but as long as forward speed is maintained (from a shallow glide), the lift equals gravity. Glide ratio — how far forward per metre of descent — varies: a sparrow ~4:1, an albatross ~20:1, a modern glider ~60:1.

**Connection to air_resistance experiment:** Air resistance (drag) and lift are both aerodynamic forces — both arise from air interacting with a surface. Streamlining reduces drag; an aerofoil shape generates lift. Aircraft design is an engineering trade-off between minimising drag and maximising lift.

**Historical connection:** The Wright Brothers achieved powered flight on 17 December 1903 after studying birds, testing over 200 wing shapes in a wind tunnel they built themselves, and solving the control problem (how to steer and bank). They were bicycle mechanics, not trained engineers — which is why they thought practically about what actually worked rather than starting from theory.

### Bloom-level questions by age

| Age | Question | Bloom level |
|---|---|---|
| 8–9 | "In Bernoulli's principle, does faster-moving air have higher or lower pressure?" | 1 — Remember |
| 10–11 | "A vacuum cleaner creates fast-moving air. If you hold a ping pong ball near the intake, will it be sucked in or pushed away? What about near the exhaust?" | 3 — Apply |
| 11–12 | "A Formula 1 car uses wings mounted upside-down. Why? What effect are they trying to achieve and how does it help?" | 4 — Analyse |
| 12–13 | "Commercial aeroplanes sometimes stall — the wings stop generating lift. This happens at low speed with a steep angle of attack. Why does reducing speed cause the wing to stop generating lift?" | 4–5 — Analyse/Evaluate |
| 13+ | "Design a paper aeroplane that maximises glide ratio (distance per metre dropped). What wing shape, aspect ratio, and angle of attack would you choose? How would you test whether a change improved it?" | 6 — Create |

## The Bigger Picture Questions

After the practical, connect outward:

- **To air_resistance experiment:** "A streamlined shape reduces drag. A wing shape generates lift. Could the same shape do both — reduce drag and generate lift? What would that shape need to look like?"
- **To engineering history:** "The Wright Brothers solved three problems: lift, propulsion, and control. Which do you think was hardest? (Most people think lift — actually it was control. Early flying machines crashed because of control problems, not lack of lift.)"
- **To sport:** "A spinning cricket ball curves in the air (swing bowling). A spinning tennis ball dips faster than gravity alone. Why does spin affect the path through the air? (Hint: Magnus effect — the spinning surface drags air around it, changing pressure on each side.)"
- **To weather:** "Wind is moving air. A weather system with low pressure at the centre causes air to flow inward. Why do you think low pressure is associated with storms, while high pressure is associated with calm weather?"
- **To biology:** "Birds, bats, and insects all evolved wings independently. All of them use the same aerodynamic principles. Why do you think all flying creatures converged on the same solution?"
