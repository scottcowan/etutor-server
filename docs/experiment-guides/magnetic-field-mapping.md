# Magnetic Field Mapping — Experiment Guide

**Curriculum topic:** `exp_magnetic_field_mapping`
**Age:** Y3–8 (ages 8–13)
**Equipment needed:** One or two strong bar magnets or horseshoe magnets (available cheaply from hardware stores or toy kits), a compass (a cheap pocket compass or a compass app on a phone held face-up works), a large sheet of white paper, a pencil, optionally iron filings in a sealed plastic bag (for dramatic effect), optionally a second magnet for field interaction

---

## What the Child Is Testing

| Test | What to map | What it reveals |
|---|---|---|
| **Single bar magnet field** | Compass direction at 20–30 points around the magnet | Field lines emerge from north pole, arc around, re-enter at south pole |
| **Field strength vs distance** | How far from the magnet does the compass deflect? | Inverse-square relationship — field strength drops sharply with distance |
| **Two magnets attracting** | Field between two opposing poles | Field lines connect pole-to-pole — attraction |
| **Two magnets repelling** | Field between two same poles | Field lines push away from each other — fields can compress and repel |

**What you'll see:** A series of curved lines — densest near the poles (strongest field) and spreading out around the sides. This is the magnetic field made visible through the effect it has on the compass needle.

---

## Tutor Script — Predict, Observe, Explain

### Before starting

Ask one predictive question before any compass is placed:

- "If I asked you to draw what a magnetic field looks like — what shape would you draw? Have you ever seen a magnetic field? Can you see it with your eyes?"
- "A compass always points north. Why? What is making the needle move — where does that force come from?"
- "Magnets can attract and repel without touching. How is that possible? What is going through the space between them?"

### Mapping the single magnet

Place the bar magnet in the centre of the paper. Trace its outline. Now place the compass at one edge of the paper, far from the magnet. Mark a dot at the tip of the compass needle. Move the compass so its tail sits on that dot. Mark a new dot at the tip. Keep moving and marking — you're tracing a field line.

Repeat starting from 6–8 different positions around the magnet.

1. "What shape are the lines? Where do they start and end?"
2. "Near the poles the lines are close together. Far from the magnet they spread out. What do you think that means about the field strength?"
3. "The compass needle points along the field line. What does that tell you about what a compass is actually measuring when it points north?"

### Testing field strength vs distance

Hold the compass at various distances from one pole: 20 cm, 15 cm, 10 cm, 5 cm, 2 cm. Observe how much the needle deflects from its normal north-pointing position.

1. "At what distance does the magnet start to have a noticeable effect on the compass?"
2. "If you halve the distance, does the deflection double? Or does it change by more or less than that?"
3. "Is the field stronger at the poles or at the sides of the magnet? How can you tell?"

### Two magnets

Place two magnets with opposite poles facing (they attract). Map the field between them with the compass.

Then flip one magnet so same poles face (they repel). Map that field.

1. "What's different about the field lines when the magnets attract vs repel?"
2. "When two magnets repel, where does the field go — does it disappear, or does it go somewhere?"
3. "If you push two repelling magnets together, where is the energy going? Where does it come from when you release them?"

### The physics connections

**What a magnetic field is:** A magnetic field is a region of space where magnetic forces act on other magnets or on moving electrical charges. It's real — not a metaphor. It has a direction (which the compass reads) and a strength (which decreases with distance). Field lines are a human invention to visualise something invisible, but they represent something physically real.

**Why magnets have poles:** At the atomic level, most atoms have electrons orbiting the nucleus. A moving charge creates a magnetic field. In most materials, these tiny atomic magnetic fields point in random directions and cancel out. In magnetic materials (iron, nickel, cobalt), something different happens: quantum mechanical effects cause atoms to "prefer" aligning in the same direction. In a permanent magnet, large numbers of atoms are aligned — their tiny fields add up to one large field.

**North and south:** The Earth itself is a giant magnet — the outer core is liquid iron churning as the Earth rotates, creating currents that generate an enormous magnetic field. A compass needle is a small magnet that aligns with Earth's field. "Magnetic north" is not the geographic North Pole — it shifts over time as the core churns. The North Pole of a compass needle points toward Earth's magnetic south pole (which is near geographic north). The naming convention is historical and mildly confusing — "north-seeking pole" became shortened to "north pole."

**Field lines and strength:** Field lines bunch together where the field is strong (near poles) and spread out where it's weak. This is true for all fields — gravitational, electric, and magnetic. Where field lines are dense, the field is strong.

**Connection to electromagnet:** The electromagnet experiment shows that moving electricity (current in a wire) creates a magnetic field — the same kind of field as a permanent magnet. This is not a coincidence. All magnetism comes from moving electric charges. A permanent magnet's field comes from the quantum spin of electrons (a quantum form of "movement"). An electromagnet's field comes from electrons moving through wire. The same fundamental origin.

**Connection to motors and generators:** Every electric motor works by using magnetic fields to push or pull coils of wire carrying current. Every generator works in reverse — moving a coil through a magnetic field to produce current. The motor in every fan, pump, and electric vehicle is a direct application of what you're mapping here.

### Bloom-level questions by age

| Age | Question | Bloom level |
|---|---|---|
| 8–9 | "What are the two poles of a magnet called? Which poles attract, and which repel?" | 1 — Remember |
| 10–11 | "If you cut a bar magnet in half, what happens? Do you get a north half and a south half, or do you get two magnets each with two poles? Why?" | 3 — Apply (the answer: two new magnets, each with N and S poles — because the source is in atomic alignment, not in "halves") |
| 11–12 | "Earth's magnetic field protects us from the solar wind — charged particles blasted off the sun. How does a magnetic field deflect moving charged particles? What would happen to Earth's surface without that protection?" | 4 — Analyse |
| 12–13 | "A compass works on a ship to navigate. But a ship's steel hull is magnetic. What problem does this create, and how do you think it could be corrected?" | 4–5 — Analyse/Evaluate |
| 13+ | "Design an experiment to test whether a fridge magnet and a rare-earth magnet produce the same shape of field or just different strengths. What measurements would you take, and what would a 'same shape, different strength' result look like?" | 6 — Create |

## The Bigger Picture Questions

After the practical, connect outward:

- **To electromagnet experiment:** "In the electromagnet experiment, a current in a coil creates a field just like this one. How do you think turning off the current changes the field? What does that tell you about the relationship between electricity and magnetism?"
- **To Earth science:** "Earth's magnetic poles have reversed many times in geological history — we know from rocks that were magnetised when they formed. Why would a polar reversal be dangerous for life on Earth today? (Hint: solar wind, satellites, compasses.)"
- **To navigation history:** "The compass was invented in China around 200 BC and spread to Europe via Arab navigators around 1100 AD. What became possible for European explorers once they had reliable compasses? What couldn't they know without GPS?"
- **To materials science:** "Rare-earth magnets (neodymium) are 10x stronger per gram than iron magnets. They're in every hard drive, electric motor, and loudspeaker. Where does the neodymium come from, and why is it geopolitically important?"
- **To medicine:** "MRI scanners use enormously powerful magnetic fields (1.5–3 Tesla, vs a fridge magnet at 0.001 Tesla) to image the inside of your body without radiation. How does a magnetic field reveal soft tissue that X-rays can't see?"
