# Candle Science — Experiment Guide

**Curriculum topic:** `exp_candle_science`
**Age:** Y3–8 (ages 8–13)
**Equipment needed:** One or more candles (birthday candles work well), matches or lighter (adult-only), a glass jar or drinking glass, a plate, water, a piece of cardboard or coin for snuffing, optionally a second jar and a damp cloth

---

## What the Child Is Testing

This experiment has three phases, each testing one aspect of combustion:

| Phase | What you observe | What it reveals |
|---|---|---|
| **Phase 1 — How long does a candle burn in a sealed jar?** | A flame goes out in a sealed container after a predictable time | Fire requires oxygen; the candle consumes all available O2 before extinguishing |
| **Phase 2 — Does jar size change burn time?** | Larger jar → longer burn time | More air = more oxygen = more fuel for combustion |
| **Phase 3 — What does the flame produce?** | Soot on cold glass; condensation (water droplets) on cold glass held above flame | Combustion produces carbon (soot), carbon dioxide, and water as products |

**What's actually happening:** Wax (a hydrocarbon — long chains of carbon and hydrogen atoms) reacts with oxygen. The reaction produces carbon dioxide and water and releases heat and light. The wax doesn't burn as a solid — it melts up the wick, vaporises, and it's the wax vapour that burns.

---

## Safety Notes (Read with Adult Before Starting)

- An adult must light and snuff the candle. Child observes, predicts, and records.
- Never leave a burning candle unattended.
- Place the candle on a plate, not directly on a table.
- Do not use a metal container — glass allows observation and won't conduct heat to hands.
- If the experiment is done with hot glass (from above the flame), use oven gloves.
- Have a damp cloth ready to smother the flame in any accident.

## Tutor Script — Predict, Observe, Explain

### Before lighting

Ask one predictive question before the adult lights the candle:

- "What do you think a candle needs in order to burn? Besides wax and a wick."
- "If I put a glass jar over this candle and sealed it, what do you think would happen? How long would the flame last — a few seconds, or a few minutes?"
- "What do you think a flame is made of? Is it a gas, a liquid, or something else?"

### Phase 1 — Jar snuff

Light the candle. Let the child observe it burning freely for 30 seconds. Then place a glass jar over it (mouth-down, resting on the plate). Time how long the flame burns before it goes out.

1. "What happened? Why do you think it went out?"
2. "The jar still looks the same from the outside — but what changed inside?"
3. "Where did the oxygen go? What do you think it turned into?"

### Phase 2 — Different jar sizes

Repeat with two different jar sizes (a small glass vs a large jar). Time each.

1. "Which jar kept the flame alive longer? Was that what you predicted?"
2. "What does this tell us about what fire needs to keep going?"
3. "If there's no oxygen left, can you get the flame going again just by lighting it? Try — what happens?" (The candle won't relight inside the jar with depleted oxygen.)

### Phase 3 — What does combustion produce?

While the candle burns freely on the plate, hold a cold piece of glass or a cold metal spoon in the flame for 2–3 seconds (use tongs — adult does this). Observe:
- Black soot on the underside
- Tiny water droplets near the edge of the soot zone

1. "What's the black stuff? Where did it come from?"
2. "What about the tiny water droplets — does that surprise you? What do you think fire is doing to make water?"
3. "So if burning wax makes soot (carbon) and water — what must be inside the wax to start with?"

### The chemistry connections

**What fire is:** Combustion is a rapid chemical reaction between a fuel and oxygen. The flame itself is superheated gas — you're seeing light and heat released as chemical bonds break and reform. The wax vapour molecules (hydrocarbons — CH₂ units) combine with O2 to form CO2 and H2O. This is why candles "disappear" — the wax is being converted to invisible gases and released into the air.

**Oxygen depletion:** The air in the jar is about 21% oxygen. The candle consumes O2 and produces CO2. When O2 drops below about 16%, combustion can't sustain itself. The CO2 produced doesn't support combustion — and CO2 is denser than air, so it settles around the base of the flame and snuffs it from below.

**Soot = incomplete combustion:** Perfect combustion produces only CO2 and water. Soot (black carbon) forms when combustion is incomplete — not enough oxygen to burn all the carbon fully. Yellow flames produce more soot than blue flames (gas hob), because yellow = hotter, sootier, less efficient combustion.

**Wax as a fuel:** Candle wax is a hydrocarbon — the same chemical family as petrol, diesel, and natural gas. The carbon and hydrogen atoms in the wax are what gets oxidised. The wick isn't fuel — it draws up liquid wax through capillary action and vaporises it at the tip.

**Connection to respiration:** Respiration (how your body gets energy from food) is essentially slow combustion. You breathe in oxygen, your cells combine it with glucose (a carbon-hydrogen compound), and the products are CO2 (which you breathe out) and water (which you lose through sweat and breath). The same reaction — just controlled, slow, and happening in every cell in your body.

### Bloom-level questions by age

| Age | Question | Bloom level |
|---|---|---|
| 8–9 | "What three things does a fire need to keep burning?" (heat, fuel, oxygen — the fire triangle) | 1–2 — Remember/Understand |
| 10–11 | "A candle in a large sealed jar burns longer than in a small jar. Does a candle in the open burn forever? Why not?" | 3 — Apply |
| 11–12 | "Why does a candle flicker in a draught but not go out? What is the draught doing, and what would it take for the draught to extinguish it?" | 4 — Analyse |
| 12–13 | "Carbon monoxide (CO) is produced when there's not enough oxygen for complete combustion. How is CO different from CO2? Why is CO dangerous?" | 4–5 — Analyse/Evaluate |
| 13+ | "Candles, wood fires, and gas hobs all use combustion. Design a campfire setup that produces minimal soot. What variables would you control, and why?" | 6 — Create |

## The Bigger Picture Questions

After the practical, connect outward:

- **To history:** "Before electricity, candles were the primary light source for thousands of years. A single candle gives about as much light as a 1-watt LED. How would that change how you spent your evenings?"
- **To atmosphere:** "Every candle you burn releases CO2. Seven billion humans burning fuels releases an enormous amount. Where does all that CO2 go — and why does it matter?"
- **To firefighting:** "Firefighters use CO2 extinguishers on electrical fires. Based on what you've seen, how does a CO2 extinguisher work? Why not water?"
- **To cooking:** "The Maillard reaction — the browning that makes toast, roast chicken, and caramelised onions taste good — is also a chemical reaction between food molecules and heat. What do you think it has in common with combustion?"
- **To breathing:** "Your cells burn glucose for energy, just like a candle burns wax. What do you breathe out that shows this is happening?"
