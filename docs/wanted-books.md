## Core History Reference

### This Sceptred Isle — Christopher Lee (BBC Books)
*The spine of the British history curriculum.*

Ten-volume narrative history of Britain from 55 BC to 1901, adapted from the
BBC Radio 4 series of the same name (216 episodes, 1995–96). Written by
Christopher Lee, narrated on radio by Anna Massey.

| Volume | Period | Key Coverage |
|---|---|---|
| Vol 1 | 55 BC–1087 | Julius Caesar, Romans, Saxons, Alfred the Great, Norman Conquest |
| Vol 2 | 1087–1327 | Plantagenets, Magna Carta, Black Death, Edward I |
| Vol 3 | 1327–1485 | Hundred Years War, Joan of Arc, Wars of the Roses |
| Vol 4 | 1485–1603 | Tudors — Henry VII, Henry VIII, Reformation, Elizabeth I, Armada |
| Vol 5 | 1603–1660 | Stuarts, English Civil War, Cromwell |
| Vol 6 | 1660–1714 | Restoration, Glorious Revolution, Bill of Rights, Union |
| Vol 7 | 1714–1789 | Hanoverians, American Revolution, Agricultural Revolution |
| Vol 8 | 1789–1837 | French Revolution, Napoleon, Industrial Revolution begins |
| Vol 9 | 1837–1880 | Victorian Britain, Reform Acts, Empire expansion, Crimea |
| Vol 10 | 1880–1901 | Height of Empire, Boer War, death of Victoria |

**Also in the series:**
- *This Sceptred Isle: The Twentieth Century* — 1901 to 2000 (2000)
- *This Sceptred Isle: Empire* — 90-part BBC Radio 4 series on the British Empire (2005–6), narrated by Juliet Stevenson

**Use for:** Populating the tutor's narrative context for any British history topic,
session summaries, and read-along passages. The conversational, anecdote-driven
style translates well to Socratic tutoring.

**ISBN (box set):** 978-0563387787 — available secondhand

## Connections and How the World Works

### Connections — James Burke (BBC Books / Little, Brown, 1978)
*The foundational text for the Connections curriculum section.*

Companion book to the BBC TV series (1978). Ten chains tracing unexpected links
between historical events and modern technology. The series and book show children
that history is not a sequence of isolated events — it is a web where pulling one
thread moves things far away in time and place.

**Chains most relevant to the curriculum:**

| Episode | Chain | Curriculum connection |
|---|---|---|
| 1: The Trigger Effect | Depends on interconnected systems; one failure cascades | `connection_telegraph_internet` |
| 2: Death in the Morning | Plough → heavy cavalry → stirrup → feudalism → universities | `connection_stirrup_feudalism`, `connection_plough_university` |
| 3: Distant Voices | Telegraph → telephone → radio → radar → microwave oven | `connection_telegraph_internet` |
| 4: Faith in Numbers | Abacus → accounting → double-entry bookkeeping → capitalism | `connection_clock_navigation_capitalism` |
| 5: The Wheel of Fortune | Mechanical clock → navigation → longitude → empire | `connection_clock_navigation_capitalism` |
| 6: Thunder in the Skies | Plough → cannon → ballistics → calculus → computers | `connection_cannon_calculus` |
| 7: The Long Chain | Dye → synthetic chemistry → drugs → penicillin | `connection_dye_medicine` |
| 8: Eat, Drink, and Be Merry | Glass → telescope → microscope → germ theory | `connection_glass_telescope_microscope` |
| 9: Countdown | Printing press → Reformation → scientific revolution | `connection_printing_revolution` |
| 10: Yesterday, Tomorrow, and You | All chains converge on the question of who controls systems | All connections topics |

**Also in the series:**
- *Connections 2* (1994) — ten more chains
- *Connections 3* (1997) — ten more
- *The Day the Universe Changed* (1985) — eight episodes on ideas that transformed how humans understand the world (evolution, germ theory, printing, industrial science, psychology, relativism)

**Use for:** The `_CONNECTIONS` curriculum section — each episode is a 45-minute source
of session narrative hooks. The tutor can open a Connections topic with a teaser:
"In 1066, a horse's foot changed the whole of European history — want to know how?"

**The Way Things Work — David Macaulay (Dorling Kindersley, 1988)**
Illustrated reference for how machines, materials, and technology work. Covers
levers, pulleys, engines, electricity, nuclear power, and digital technology.
The standard reference for the `_HOW_THINGS_ARE_MADE` and `_MATERIALS` curriculum
sections. Ages 8–14.
Updated as *The New Way Things Work* (1998) to include digital technology.

**The Disappearing Spoon — Sam Kean (Little, Brown, 2010)**
A story for each element of the periodic table. Covers the discovery, properties,
and human stories of every element — mercury poisoning, the nuclear arms race,
the rare earth metals that power modern devices.
*Why it works:* Each chapter is self-contained and 15–20 minutes. Connects
`_MATERIALS` topics (aluminium, copper, rare earth, lithium) to history and human
drama. Age 12+.

**The Alchemy of Air — Thomas Haber (Crown, 2008)**
The story of Fritz Haber and the Haber process — how nitrogen fixation fed billions
but also enabled chemical warfare and the Holocaust. The most important story in
`connection_nitrogen_war_food`.
Age 13+.

**Stuff Matters — Mark Miodownik (Viking, 2013)**
A materials scientist explores ten everyday materials — steel, glass, paper,
chocolate, foam, plastic, graphite, porcelain, concrete, fabric. Each chapter
is a meditation on a material's history, properties, and meaning.
*Why it works:* Short chapters, beautiful prose, and the material itself as the
narrative anchor. Maps directly to the `_MATERIALS` curriculum section.
Age 11+.

## British Empire — Deep Reference

### Empire: How Britain Made the Modern World — Niall Ferguson (Allen Lane, 2003)
Covers the economic, political, and cultural mechanisms of the Empire.
Strong on trade and finance — maps well to `empire_trade_economy` and
`british_empire_expansion` curriculum topics.
**Age suitability for excerpts:** 13+ (analytical, some adult content)

### The Scramble for Africa — Thomas Pakenham (Abacus, 1991)
Definitive account of European colonisation of Africa 1876–1912.
Useful for `empire_everyday_life` and `decolonisation` topics.

### Bury My Heart at Wounded Knee — Dee Brown (Holt, 1970)
Native American perspective on the colonisation of the Americas.
Useful for `age_of_exploration` and `empire_everyday_life` — non-European viewpoint.

### The Interesting Narrative — Olaudah Equiano (1789)
Primary source autobiography of an enslaved person who won his freedom.
Direct resource for `transatlantic_slave_trade` and `abolition_movement`.
**Age suitability for excerpts:** 10+ (with guidance)

## Children's Fiction — Reading Samples

Books selected for read-along sessions. Criteria: strong narrative voice that
works aloud, passages that invite Socratic questions, age-appropriate vocabulary,
and curriculum crossover where possible. Organised by age group.

Each entry includes: what makes it good for read-along, which curriculum topics
it connects to, and example Socratic questions the tutor could ask mid-passage.

---

### Ages 6–7 (Y1–2)

**The Tiger Who Came to Tea — Judith Kerr (HarperCollins, 1968)**
A tiger knocks on the door and eats everything in the house.
*Why it works:* Short sentences, clear story structure, wonderful cause-and-effect
for early comprehension. Memorable enough that children can recall and retell it.
*Questions:* "What do you think will happen when Daddy gets home?" / "Why do you
think the tiger ate everything?"

**The Gruffalo — Julia Donaldson (Macmillan, 1999)**
A mouse outwits predators by inventing a monster — who turns out to be real.
*Why it works:* Rhyme and rhythm support decoding. Irony and reversal at the end
is Bloom level 3 thinking accessible to age 6.
*Questions:* "Do you think the mouse is brave or clever?" / "What would you do
if you met the Gruffalo?"

**The Owl Who Was Afraid of the Dark — Jill Tomlinson (Methuen, 1968)**
Baby barn owl Plop meets different people who each show him something good about
the dark. Chapter book — good for multi-session read-along.
*Why it works:* Each chapter is self-contained, episodic, and introduces a new
adult character with a different perspective. Natural conversation starter.
*Questions:* "Why do you think Plop is scared of the dark?" / "Who has the most
convincing reason that dark is good — the boy scout, the old lady, or the
astronomer?"

**Fantastic Mr Fox — Roald Dahl (Allen & Unwin, 1970)**
Mr Fox outsmarts three horrible farmers to feed his family.
*Why it works:* Short chapters, vivid characters with clear motivations, strong
moral dimension (theft justified by survival) that children love to debate.
*Questions:* "Is Mr Fox a good fox or a bad fox?" / "Why are Boggis, Bunce, and
Bean so horrible?"

**Paddington Bear — Michael Bond (Collins, 1958)**
A bear from Peru arrives at Paddington station and is taken in by the Brown family.
*Why it works:* Immigration and belonging handled warmly. Connects to `windrush_immigration`
and `continents_oceans`. Paddington's foreignness is played for gentle comedy, not threat.
*Questions:* "How would you feel if you arrived somewhere new and didn't know anyone?"
/ "What makes somewhere feel like home?"

---

### Ages 8–9 (Y3–4)

**Charlie and the Chocolate Factory — Roald Dahl (Allen & Unwin, 1964)**
Charlie Bucket wins a golden ticket to Wonka's mysterious factory.
*Why it works:* Vivid descriptive language, sharp social commentary about greed and
poverty that rewards analysis. Charlie's family poverty is genuinely moving.
*Questions:* "Why does Dahl make Charlie's family so poor?" / "Which child deserves
to win and why?"

**The Iron Man — Ted Hughes (Faber & Faber, 1968)**
A giant iron man appears from nowhere and terrorises a farming community.
*Why it works:* Mythic structure, ecological themes, and an extraordinary ending where
destruction is transformed into something beautiful. Short chapters, rich language.
Connects to `forces_magnets` and `materials_ks1`.
*Questions:* "What do you think the Iron Man is — robot, alien, or something else?"
/ "Why does Hogarth decide to trust him?"

**Charlotte's Web — E.B. White (Harper & Brothers, 1952)**
A spider saves a pig's life by weaving words into her web.
*Why it works:* Friendship, sacrifice, and death handled with extraordinary gentleness.
The scene where Charlotte dies is one of the best in children's literature.
Connects to `living_things_ks1` and `animals_habitats`.
*Questions:* "Why does Charlotte help Wilbur even though it costs her so much?"
/ "What does 'Some Pig' actually mean?"

**The Midnight Fox — Betsy Byars (Viking, 1968)**
A city boy reluctantly spending summer on a farm becomes obsessed with a wild fox.
*Why it works:* Interior monologue that feels genuinely like a child's thinking.
Strong nature connection. Good for discussing courage under pressure.
*Questions:* "Tom doesn't want to be on the farm at all — how does that change by
the end?" / "Was Tom right to do what he did to save the fox?"

**Kensuke's Kingdom — Michael Morpurgo (Heinemann, 1999)**
A boy is stranded on a Pacific island and meets an old Japanese man living alone.
*Why it works:* WW2 backstory handled simply and powerfully. Silence and trust as
themes. Connects to `ww2_global_conflict`. Morpurgo's prose is clear and absorbing.
*Questions:* "Why won't Kensuke let Michael signal the ships?" / "What is Kensuke
afraid of — and is he right to be afraid?"

**The Lion, the Witch and the Wardrobe — C.S. Lewis (Geoffrey Bles, 1950)**
Four children enter a magical world through a wardrobe and must help defeat an evil witch.
*Why it works:* Classic story structure, clear moral framework, good evil richly
characterised. Allegory that rewards discussion at many levels.
*Questions:* "Why does Edmund betray his siblings?" / "Is the White Witch ever
right about anything?"

---

### Ages 9–11 (Y4–6)

**Goodnight Mister Tom — Michelle Magorian (Kestrel Books, 1981)**
A London boy evacuated to the countryside during WW2 is taken in by a gruff widower.
*Why it works:* The most powerful children's novel about WW2 evacuation. Deals with
child abuse, grief, and found family without sentimentality. Connects directly to
`ww2_global_conflict` and `history_changes_living`.
*Questions:* "How does Tom change through the story — and why?" / "Why does Willie
not tell anyone about what happens at home?"

**The Machine Gunners — Robert Westall (Macmillan, 1975)**
A group of children in WW2 Tyneside find a crashed German plane with its gun intact.
*Why it works:* War seen from children's perspective — exciting and morally complex.
Carnegie Medal winner. Connects to `ww2_global_conflict`.
*Questions:* "Should the children have told the adults?" / "Does the story make you
feel sorry for the German airman?"

**Roll of Thunder, Hear My Cry — Mildred D. Taylor (Dial Press, 1976)**
A Black family in 1930s Mississippi holds onto their land against racist violence.
*Why it works:* The most important book for understanding American racism from a
child's perspective. Raw but never exploitative. Connects to `civil_rights_global`
and `transatlantic_slave_trade` legacy.
*Questions:* "Why is owning the land so important to the Logan family?" / "What
does Cassie learn about the world that she didn't know at the beginning?"

**The Wonderful Wizard of Oz — L. Frank Baum (George M. Hill, 1900)**
Dorothy is swept to a magical land and must find her way home.
*Why it works:* Original text is richer than the film. Each companion's desire
(brain, heart, courage) is a gift they already possess — a powerful theme.
*Questions:* "If the Scarecrow already makes clever decisions, does he actually
need a brain?" / "What does home mean to Dorothy?"

**Treasure Island — Robert Louis Stevenson (Cassell, 1883)**
Jim Hawkins discovers a treasure map and sets sail — but the crew includes pirates.
*Why it works:* Perfect adventure structure, morally ambiguous Long John Silver is
one of literature's greatest characters. Connects to `age_of_exploration` and maritime
history. Victorian prose but very accessible.
*Questions:* "Is Long John Silver a villain?" / "Why does Jim keep going back to
him even after he knows the truth?"

**The Jungle Book — Rudyard Kipling (Macmillan, 1894)**
Mowgli is raised by wolves in the Indian jungle.
*Why it works:* Connects directly to `british_empire_expansion` and `mughal_empire`.
Kipling's India is vivid; the stories reward discussion of belonging, identity, and
whether Mowgli is more human or wolf. Worth discussing Kipling's imperial context.
*Questions:* "Does Mowgli belong in the jungle or with humans?" / "The animals have
their own law — is it fair?"
*Note:* Kipling held imperialist views. Use as an opportunity to discuss how we read
books written in a different time — what does the story assume about India?

**A Wizard of Earthsea — Ursula K. Le Guin (Parnassus Press, 1968)**
A boy with magical gifts must face the shadow he released into the world.
*Why it works:* Stunning prose, a protagonist of colour at the centre of a fantasy
world, and a psychological depth unusual in children's fantasy. The "shadow" as
confronting your own worst self is rich Bloom level 4–5 territory for ages 10+.
*Questions:* "What is the shadow actually — where does it come from?" / "Why does
Ged run from it for so long before turning to face it?"

### Ages 11–13 (Y6–8)

**To Kill a Mockingbird — Harper Lee (J.B. Lippincott, 1960)**
Scout Finch watches her lawyer father defend a Black man wrongly accused in 1930s Alabama.
*Why it works:* The best introduction to American racial injustice for young readers.
Scout's child perspective lets the reader understand injustice without being lectured at.
Connects to `civil_rights_global` and `transatlantic_slave_trade` legacy.
**Age suitability:** 11+ (racial violence, adult themes handled carefully)
*Questions:* "Why does Atticus defend Tom Robinson when he knows he will lose?"
/ "What does Boo Radley's story tell us about who gets treated as an outsider?"

**The Boy in the Striped Pyjamas — John Boyne (David Fickling, 2006)**
Bruno, son of a Nazi commandant, befriends a Jewish boy through a concentration camp fence.
*Why it works:* Bruno's deliberate naivety forces the reader to fill the horror he
cannot see. Controversial but extraordinarily effective for teaching the Holocaust.
Connects to `ww2_global_conflict`.
**Age suitability:** 11+ (Holocaust context — use with care and discussion)
*Questions:* "Why doesn't Bruno understand where he is?" / "What does the ending
tell us about the logic of what happened?"

**Skellig — David Almond (Hodder, 1998)**
A boy finds a mysterious creature in his crumbling garage while his baby sister is dying.
*Why it works:* Language is luminous, themes are mortality, wonder, and the borderline
between ordinary and extraordinary. Carnegie Medal. Reads beautifully aloud.
*Questions:* "What do you think Skellig is?" / "How does Michael change from the
beginning to the end?"

**His Dark Materials: Northern Lights — Philip Pullman (Scholastic, 1995)**
Lyra lives in an Oxford where every person has an animal daemon — their soul made visible.
*Why it works:* Extraordinary world-building, complex moral framework, science vs
religion themes handled without simplification. The best fantasy novel for this age.
Connects to `philosophy_for_children` vocational topic.
*Questions:* "What does it mean that your daemon is the part of you that's outside
your body?" / "Are the Gobblers right that cutting the daemon away would make
children better?"

**The Curious Incident of the Dog in the Night-Time — Mark Haddon (Jonathan Cape, 2003)**
Christopher, who has autism, investigates the murder of a neighbour's dog.
*Why it works:* First-person voice from an autistic protagonist is extraordinary —
teaches empathy by placing the reader inside a mind that works differently.
*Questions:* "Why does Christopher find human faces so hard to read?" / "Is
Christopher a good detective?"

**Animal Farm — George Orwell (Secker & Warburg, 1945)**
Farm animals overthrow their human farmer — but the pigs become tyrants.
*Why it works:* The most accessible political allegory ever written. Connects to
`cold_war_context`, `history_of_democracy_britain`, and `civil_rights_global`.
Language is simple; ideas are profound.
*Questions:* "At what exact moment does the revolution go wrong?" / "Are some
animals always going to end up more equal than others?"
**Age suitability:** 11+ for political allegory to land; can be read younger as
a simpler animal story

## Older Texts and Primary Sources — Reading Progression

The goal is to build children toward reading older English with confidence.
The two-pass strategy applies throughout this section: **modern retelling first,
original text later.** Each entry notes the right entry point and what the original
reveals that a retelling cannot.

Organised chronologically by era, with age guidance for both passes.

---

### Old English (~450–1100 AD)

**Beowulf (~8th century)**
The oldest substantial piece of English literature. A warrior defeats the monster
Grendel, then Grendel's mother, then a dragon in old age.
*Modern retelling first (ages 9–11):* Seamus Heaney's verse translation (Faber, 1999)
is the gold standard — powerful, accessible, reads beautifully aloud. Even excerpts
("So. The Spear-Danes in days gone by...") give a child the feel of the oral tradition.
Michael Morpurgo's prose retelling (Walker Books, 2006) is good for Y5–6.
*Original text (ages 14+):* Old English looks like a foreign language.
The point is not to read it fluently but to see *how different English was* — the
runes, the compound words (whale-road for sea, word-hoard for vocabulary).
*Questions:* "Why do the warriors keep boasting about what they've done before a
fight?" / "What does it tell us that the monsters are always attacking at night?"
*Curriculum:* `vikings_saxons`, `stone_bronze_iron_age`

---

### Middle English (1100–1500)

**The Canterbury Tales — Geoffrey Chaucer (~1390)**
A group of pilgrims travel to Canterbury and tell each other stories. Each tale
reveals the teller's personality, class, and moral view.
*Modern retelling (ages 10–12):* Geraldine McCaughrean's prose versions (Oxford,
1984) are the standard school introduction. Peter Ackroyd's prose retelling (Penguin,
2009) is richer for older readers.
*Original text (ages 13+):* Middle English is hard but not impossible. The General
Prologue's opening lines ("Whan that Aprill with his shoures soote...") can be
sounded out by a confident reader with a glossary. The experience of hearing the
language *almost* make sense is itself a lesson.
*Why it matters:* Chaucer is the first great portrait gallery of English social
life. The Wife of Bath, the Knight, the Miller — each is a type that still exists.
*Questions:* "Which pilgrim would you most want to sit next to at dinner?" / "The
Knight is supposed to be noble — does Chaucer actually like him?"
*Curriculum:* `magna_carta_barons`, `hundred_years_war`, `black_death`

**Sir Gawain and the Green Knight (~1375)**
A mysterious green giant challenges King Arthur's knights. Only Gawain accepts —
and must face the consequences a year later.
*Modern retelling (ages 10–12):* Simon Armitage's verse translation (Faber, 2007)
is outstanding — modern English that keeps the alliterative rhythm.
*Questions:* "Is Gawain a coward or honest? Can he be both?" / "What is the Green
Knight actually testing?"
*Curriculum:* `norman_conquest`, `magna_carta_barons`

---

### Early Modern English / Tudor (1500–1600)

**Shakespeare — Selected Plays**
The heart of the KS3 English curriculum. The two-pass strategy is most important here.

*Retelling first (ages 9–12):*
- Leon Garfield's *Shakespeare Stories* (Victor Gollancz, 1985) — prose retellings
  of all the major plays, beautifully written. The best single introduction.
- Marcia Williams' illustrated versions for younger children (Walker Books)
- Charles and Mary Lamb's *Tales from Shakespeare* (1807) — the Victorian standard;
  still readable, slightly dated in tone

*Original text — recommended entry sequence (ages 12+):*

| Play | Why start here | Key language challenge |
|---|---|---|
| **A Midsummer Night's Dream** | Comic, fast-moving, magic | Verse but mostly clear; "thou/thee" |
| **The Tempest** | Compact, colonial themes, spectacle | Prospero's speeches are dense |
| **Romeo and Juliet** | Story already known; strong emotion | Sonnet form in the prologue |
| **Macbeth** | Dark, short, psychologically gripping | Witches' chants; soliloquy density |
| **Hamlet** | The summit — tackle after the others | Most famous difficulty; worth the climb |

*Language notes for all Shakespeare:*
- "Thou/thee/thy" = you (singular, intimate) — explain once, child adapts quickly
- "Dost", "hast", "art" = do/have/are — verb endings that feel like a code to crack
- Obscene wordplay is everywhere. Shakespeare's puns are often sexual. Handle with
  the same calm matter-of-factness as historical language elsewhere — "that's a rude
  joke that audiences in 1600 would have laughed at"
- "Gay", "queer", "fond", "nice", "awful", "silly" all mean different things —
  good teaching moments about semantic shift

*Questions for A Midsummer Night's Dream:* "Why does Puck think humans are foolish?"
/ "Is it fair that Hermia's father can choose who she marries?"
*Questions for Macbeth:* "Who is more to blame — Macbeth or Lady Macbeth?" / "At
what point does Macbeth stop being a good person?"
*Curriculum:* `shakespeare_ks3`, `elizabethan_era`, `henry_viii_reformation`

**Christopher Marlowe — Doctor Faustus (~1592)**
A scholar sells his soul to the devil for twenty-four years of power and knowledge.
*Modern approach (ages 13+):* Read alongside Shakespeare as evidence that Elizabethan
drama was a conversation between writers. Faustus's final soliloquy ("See, see where
Christ's blood streams in the firmament!") is one of the most powerful pieces of
English verse.
*Questions:* "Was Faustus wrong to make the bargain?" / "Why does he waste his
powers on conjuring tricks instead of doing something meaningful?"
*Curriculum:* `elizabethan_era`, `henry_viii_reformation`

### 17th Century

**The King James Bible (1611)**
Taught as literature, history, and cultural literacy — not religious instruction.
The KJB is the single most influential text in the history of the English language
and the foundational narrative of Western culture. A child who doesn't know its
stories is missing the context for half of English literature, art, and idiom.

**Approach — big picture first, then zoom in:**

*Pass 1 — The overall narrative arc (ages 7–9):*
Start with the shape of the whole thing: Creation → Fall → the Patriarchs →
Egypt and the Exodus → the Kingdom of Israel → the Prophets → the Gospels →
the early Church. A child doesn't need to have read it all — they need a mental
map so that when they encounter a reference (David and Goliath, the Good Samaritan,
the Tower of Babel) they know roughly where it lives.

A simple retelling first: *The Lion Storyteller Bible* (Bob Hartman, Lion Hudson)
for ages 5–8, or *The Complete Illustrated Children's Bible* (Harvest House) for
ages 8–12. These give the narrative without the KJB's archaic register.

*Pass 2 — The stories worth zooming in on (ages 8+):*
Move to the KJB text for the most narratively rich passages. These are the ones
that have generated the most art, literature, and idiom — worth knowing in the
original language.

**Old Testament — essential stories:**

| Moses and the Exodus | Exodus 1–20 | Slavery, liberation, the law; foundational for Jewish, Christian, and Muslim traditions |
| David and Goliath | 1 Samuel 17 | The underdog story that shaped Western culture |

| Solomon's Wisdom | 1 Kings 3 | The two mothers and the baby; justice and discernment |
| Elijah and the Still Small Voice | 1 Kings 19 | Burnout, despair, and renewal — surprisingly modern |
| Daniel in the Lions' Den | Daniel 6 | Integrity under tyranny |

| The Road to Emmaus | Luke 24 | Recognition and wonder after the resurrection |
| The Letter to the Corinthians | 1 Corinthians 13 | "Though I speak with the tongues of men and of angels..." — the greatest definition of love in English |
| Revelation | Revelation 1, 12, 21–22 | Apocalyptic vision; the language of Western ideas of end and renewal |

*Questions at each story:*
- "What does this story say about what people were afraid of?"
- "Why do you think this story has been told for thousands of years?"
- "Is the person in this story right or wrong? How do you know?"
- "Can you think of a film or book that tells the same kind of story?"

*Language harvest:* After reading any passage, ask the child to spot phrases still
in everyday use. Almost every passage yields several. This builds both vocabulary
and cultural literacy simultaneously.

*Curriculum links:* `henry_viii_reformation` (Reformation was a fight over this
text), `english_civil_war` (Cromwell's Puritans saturated in it), `transatlantic_slave_trade`
(both used to justify and to oppose it), `abolition_movement` (Wilberforce and
Equiano both drew heavily on it), `shakespeare_ks3` (Shakespeare quotes or
alludes to the Bible on almost every page), `philosophy_for_children`

*Note on religious sensitivity:* Treat the Bible the way you treat Homer or
Ovid — as a foundational literary and cultural text, not as doctrine. The tutor
does not advocate belief or disbelief. When a child asks "is it true?", the
honest answer is: "Many people believe it is. The stories matter whether or not
you do, because they shaped the world we live in."

**John Milton — Paradise Lost (1667)**
Satan falls from Heaven and corrupts Adam and Eve. The grandest poem in English.
*Approach (ages 14+):* Selective. Books I and II (Satan in Hell) are the most
dramatic and accessible. Milton's Satan is so compelling that William Blake
said Milton was "of the Devil's party without knowing it."
*Questions:* "Does Satan seem heroic at the start?" / "Why does Milton make the
villain so much more interesting than God?"
*Curriculum:* `english_civil_war`, `glorious_revolution`

### 18th Century

**Robinson Crusoe — Daniel Defoe (1719)**
A man shipwrecked on a desert island builds a new life alone — then discovers
a footprint.
*Modern retelling first (ages 9–11):* Many simplified versions available.
*Original text (ages 12+):* Early novel prose — very readable by modern standards.
A good first "adult" novel for confident readers.
*Language/context note:* Crusoe's relationship with Friday is deeply colonial.
The text assumes the right to enslave and rename. Use the same approach as
The Jungle Book — discuss what the text assumes without destroying the adventure.
*Questions:* "What would you do first if you were stranded alone?" / "Is Crusoe
right to take charge of Friday?"
*Curriculum:* `age_of_exploration`, `british_empire_expansion`

**Gulliver's Travels — Jonathan Swift (1726)**
Lemuel Gulliver visits lands of giants, tiny people, flying islands, and talking horses.
*Modern retelling first (ages 8–10):* The Lilliput sections read as straightforward
adventure. Most children's editions stop there.
*Original text (ages 12+):* Swift is savagely satirising English politics and human
nature throughout. The Houyhnhnms section (rational horses who find humans disgusting)
is uncomfortable and profound.
*Questions:* "Why does Gulliver feel ashamed of being human by the end?" / "Which
society in the book is closest to England in Swift's time?"
*Curriculum:* `glorious_revolution`, `industrial_revolution`

### 19th Century — Victorian Prose

**A Christmas Carol — Charles Dickens (1843)**
Scrooge is visited by three ghosts on Christmas Eve and shown the consequences of greed.
*Why it works:* Short, very fast-moving, Dickens at his most accessible.
Directly connects to `victorian_britain` and the industrial poverty it documents.
*Original text (ages 9+):* Victorian prose but very readable. "Bah! Humbug!" is

*Language note:* "Queer" and "gay" appear in their older senses throughout Dickens.
*Questions:* "Why does Scrooge hate Christmas?" / "Is he a bad man or just a

*Curriculum:* `victorian_britain`, `industrial_revolution`

**Alice's Adventures in Wonderland — Lewis Carroll (1865)**
Alice falls down a rabbit hole into a world of logic puzzles and absurd characters.
*Why it works:* Perfect read-aloud text — rhythm, wordplay, absurdism. Every chapter

*Original text (ages 8+):* Victorian but entirely accessible. The puns require

*Questions:* "Is Wonderland a dream or a real place — does it matter?" / "Why does

*Curriculum:* `reading_comprehension_ks2`, `victorian_britain`

**Treasure Island — Robert Louis Stevenson (1883)**
*(See Children's Fiction section — listed again here for the language progression

**The Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde — Stevenson (1886)**

*Ages 12+.* Short (really a novella). The language of Victorian science and

becoming Hyde even though he hates what Hyde does?"
*Curriculum:* `victorian_britain`, `industrial_revolution`

## Art, Visual Language, and Comics

### Understanding Comics — Scott McCloud (Kitchen Sink Press, 1993)
*The most important book for teaching visual literacy.*

McCloud explains how comics work as a visual language — not just as entertainment
but as a system for communicating ideas through pictures. Written entirely as a comic
itself, it demonstrates its own arguments.

**The key ideas, each teachable:**

| Concept | What it teaches | Curriculum connection |
|---|---|---|
| **The Vocabulary of Icons** | A smiley face carries more universal meaning than a photograph — the simpler the image, the more you project yourself onto it | `art_appreciation_visual_literacy` — why abstraction works |
| **The Picture Plane** | A spectrum from pure representation (a photograph) to pure language (the letter A) with every artwork somewhere on it | `art_composition_perspective`, `art_colour_theory_ks2` |
| **Closure** | The reader's brain fills in what happens between panels — we are active meaning-makers, not passive receivers | `reading_comprehension_ks2` — inference and deduction |
| **Time and Motion in Space** | How a static image implies movement; how panel layout controls pacing | `art_history_ancient_renaissance` — medieval manuscripts, Egyptian frieze |
| **Blood in the Gutter** | Violence in comics is completed in the reader's imagination — the reader is complicit | `media_literacy_ks3`, `advertising_persuasion` — how media implicates the audience |
| **The Six Transitions** | Different ways one panel can follow another — moment-to-moment, action-to-action, subject-to-subject, scene-to-scene, aspect-to-aspect, non-sequitur | Story structure and narrative pacing |
| **Colour Symbolism** | How colour carries emotional meaning across cultures | `art_colour_theory_ks2` — warm/cool, psychological associations |
| **Word-Image Relationships** | Seven ways words and images can relate — each creating different meaning | Reading comprehension, media literacy |

**Why it works on an e-ink device:** The book is page-by-page and entirely textual
in argument, with illustrations. Passages read aloud beautifully — McCloud's prose
style is clear and enthusiastic. The Socratic questions write themselves:

- "Why do you think a simple cartoon face feels more like YOU than a photograph?"
- "McCloud says you are the killer in a violent comic — what do you think he means?"
- "Look at a road sign and a traffic light — where do they sit on the picture plane?"

**Age suitability:** The ideas are accessible from age 9. The full book reads at
age 12+. Selected concepts work well from Year 4 upwards.

**Use for:**
- Opening `art_appreciation_visual_literacy` — McCloud's picture plane is the best
  single framework for talking about abstraction and realism
- `media_literacy_ks3` — blood in the gutter / audience complicity
- `advertising_persuasion` — iconic vs realistic representation in ads
- `art_colour_theory_ks2` — colour symbolism chapter

**Also in the series:**
- *Reinventing Comics* (2000) — the future of comics as a medium; digital storytelling
- *Making Comics* (2006) — practical guide to creating visual narratives

**Related:**
- *Comics and Sequential Art* — Will Eisner (1985): the other founding text; more
  technical, less philosophical; good for older learners interested in making comics
- *The Complete Maus* — Art Spiegelman: the highest literary achievement in comics;
  Holocaust history told through animal metaphor. Uses McCloud's vocabulary throughout.
  Age 12+. Maps to `ww2_global_conflict` and `transatlantic_slave_trade` legacy.
- *Persepolis* — Marjane Satrapi: Iranian revolution through a child's eyes. Age 12+.
  Maps to `authoritarianism_types`, `censorship_history`.
- *Fun Home* — Alison Bechdel: memoir in comics form. Age 14+. Maps to
  `sexuality_relationships`, `identity_who_am_i`, `shakespeare_ks3` (Bechdel was
  obsessed with Ulysses).

## Cultural Capital and Class

### Watching the English — Kate Fox (Hodder, 2004)
The single best guide to how the English actually behave. An anthropologist
applies field research methods to English social rituals — the pub, the queue,
the weather conversation, the class markers embedded in vocabulary and behaviour.
*Why it matters:* The book decodes the unwritten rules explicitly, which is exactly
what the Cultural Capital curriculum section aims to do. Fox names the class signals
that most English people absorb without realising.
*Use for:* `british_class_system`, `class_and_language`, `accent_class_signal`,
`formal_dining_etiquette`

### Watching the English: The Hidden Rules of English Behaviour — Kate Fox
Same book, different note: the chapters on "class" and "talk" are the most directly

### Snobs — Julian Fellowes (Weidenfeld & Nicolson, 2004)
A novel about class mobility and the British aristocracy by the creator of

Memoir of a woman raised without formal education in rural Idaho who eventually

## Dissent and Authority

### Civil Disobedience — Henry David Thoreau (1849)
Short essay (pamphlet length) that founded the theory of civil disobedience.
"The only obligation which I have a right to assume is to do at any time what
I think right." Gandhi and Martin Luther King Jr both cite it as foundational.
*Use for:* `civil_disobedience_theory`, `legitimate_vs_illegitimate_authority`
**Age suitability:** 12+ (short enough to read in full)

### The Milgram Experiment — Stanley Milgram (Harper & Row, 1974)
Milgram's own account of his obedience studies. More accessible than the
academic papers. The chapter describing individual participant reactions
is among the most disturbing social science writing ever published.
*Use for:* `milgram_asch_obedience`, `authority_and_obedience`
**Age suitability:** 13+

### The Anatomy of Fascism — Robert Paxton (Knopf, 2004)
The most rigorous analysis of what fascism actually is (a movement and behaviour,
not an ideology) and how authoritarian movements come to power. Directly relevant
to `pattern_authoritarian_rise`.
*Use for:* `pattern_authoritarian_rise`, `authority_and_obedience`
**Age suitability:** 14+

### The Spy Who Came in from the Cold — John le Carré (Gollancz, 1963)
The best Cold War spy novel — and a profound examination of institutional loyalty,
moral compromise, and what happens when the rules of "our side" turn out to be
as bad as the other side's. Fiction that teaches the Milgram lesson through story.
*Use for:* `whistleblowers`, `legitimate_vs_illegitimate_authority`, `cold_war_context`
**Age suitability:** 13+

### Permanent Record — Edward Snowden (Metropolitan Books, 2019)
Snowden's own account of how and why he leaked NSA surveillance programmes.
Whatever your view of his decision, it is one of the most detailed first-person
accounts of the whistleblower's dilemma — institutional loyalty vs. conscience.
*Use for:* `whistleblowers`, `cia_covert_history`
**Age suitability:** 13+

### The Rights of Man — Thomas Paine (1791)
Written in response to Burke's defence of the French monarchy. Paine's argument
that rights are inherent to persons, not granted by governments, is the foundation
of modern human rights theory. Connects to both the Dissent curriculum and the
Political Systems section.
*Use for:* `civil_disobedience_theory`, `history_of_dissent`,
`history_of_democracy_britain`
**Age suitability:** 12+ for excerpts; accessible prose for the period

## Surveillance, Privacy, and the Watched State

### 1984 — George Orwell (Secker & Warburg, 1949)
The foundational text. Orwell's surveillance state as a model for understanding
real ones — and as a vocabulary (Big Brother, Newspeak, doublethink, Thoughtcrime,
Room 101) that has become part of the language. Should be read before or alongside
the Surveillance curriculum section.
*Use for:* `orwell_1984`, `what_is_surveillance`, `pattern_propaganda`,
`censorship_history`
**Age suitability:** 12+ (dark but not gratuitously so; the torture in Part 3
is disturbing but important)
*Also:* Orwell's essay "Politics and the English Language" (1946) — the best
short guide to how language is used to obscure rather than illuminate, directly
relevant to `propaganda_techniques` and `vocab_power_of_words`.

### The Age of Surveillance Capitalism — Shoshana Zuboff (Profile Books, 2019)
The definitive analysis of how Google, Facebook, and the attention economy work.
Dense but important — even reading the first three chapters gives children the
framework for understanding why "free" services aren't free.
*Use for:* `surveillance_capitalism`, `attention_economy`, `data_science_concepts`
**Age suitability for excerpts:** 14+; the core argument can be summarised
for younger children

### No Place to Hide — Glenn Greenwald (Metropolitan Books, 2014)
Greenwald's account of working with Snowden to publish the NSA documents.
The most readable account of what PRISM and Tempora actually collected and why
it mattered. Short chapters, journalistic prose.
*Use for:* `gchq_and_nsa`, `whistleblowers`, `privacy_as_right`
**Age suitability:** 13+

### The Lives of Others (film, 2006) — Florian Henckel von Donnersmarck
Not a book, but the best dramatisation of the Stasi surveillance state.
A Stasi officer assigned to monitor a playwright discovers his own humanity
in the process of observing his subject's. Won the Academy Award for Best
Foreign Language Film. The most emotionally effective way to understand
what it meant to live under the East German surveillance system.
*Use for:* `stasi_files`, `soviet_surveillance`, `orwell_1984`
**Age suitability:** 12+ (some adult content, mostly intense rather than explicit)

### Data and Goliath — Bruce Schneier (W.W. Norton, 2015)
Security technologist's account of surveillance by both states and corporations.
More practical and less theoretical than Zuboff — explains the actual systems

*Use for:* `gchq_and_nsa`, `surveillance_capitalism`, `resisting_surveillance`
**Age suitability for excerpts:** 13+

### Nothing to Hide — Daniel Solove (Yale University Press, 2011)
A law professor's comprehensive demolition of the "nothing to hide" argument.
Short enough to read in excerpts; systematic in its reasoning.
*Use for:* `privacy_as_right`, `surveillance_capitalism`
**Age suitability for excerpts:** 13+

### Physics

**The Feynman Lectures on Physics — Richard P. Feynman, Robert B. Leighton & Matthew Sands (Caltech/Basic Books; 3 vols, 1963; new millennium edition 2010)**
The most intellectually alive undergraduate physics text ever written.
Volume I covers mechanics, radiation, heat, and wave behaviour; Volume II covers
electromagnetism and matter; Volume III covers quantum mechanics.
Feynman's explanations are constructed from first principles with no shortcuts —
every result has a reason. The lecture style translates naturally to Socratic tutoring:
Feynman asks *why* at every step. Now freely available online at feynmanlectures.caltech.edu.
*Use for:* All physics curriculum topics — the tutor can quote directly from Feynman's
plain-English derivations and analogies. Particularly strong on electromagnetism,
quantum, and thermodynamics.
**Library:** Kavita — reference
**Age group:** Lesson source for ages 10–12 (upper KS3 and beyond)

**Fundamentals of Physics — David Halliday, Robert Resnick & Jearl Walker (Wiley; 10th edition 2013)**
The canonical first-year university physics textbook worldwide. Comprehensive coverage
of mechanics, thermodynamics, electromagnetism, optics, and modern physics, with
worked examples, review questions, and clear derivations. Less colourful than
Feynman but more systematic — good for locating a clean statement of a law or result.
*Use for:* Any physics topic requiring a textbook-standard formulation. Good complement
to Feynman: use Feynman for intuition, Halliday/Resnick for the systematic worked example.
**Library:** Kavita — reference
**Age group:** Lesson source for ages 11–12

**Relativity: The Special and the General Theory — Albert Einstein (Methuen, 1920; many reprints)**
Einstein's own popular account of special and general relativity, written for readers
without advanced mathematics. Primary source and model of how to explain a revolutionary
idea from first principles. The opening chapters on the relativity of simultaneity are
among the clearest pages of science writing in existence.
*Use for:* `relativity_special`, `relativity_general`, `speed_of_light`, `history_of_physics`
**Library:** Kavita — reference
**Age group:** Lesson source for ages 10–12

**Introduction to Quantum Mechanics — David J. Griffiths (Cambridge; 3rd edition 2018)**
The standard undergraduate quantum mechanics text. Covers wave-particle duality,
the Schrödinger equation, the hydrogen atom, spin, and the measurement problem.
Griffiths is a gifted explainer; the text is notable for its lack of pretension and
its willingness to acknowledge what quantum mechanics actually means.
*Use for:* `quantum_mechanics`, `wave_particle_duality`, `atomic_structure`,
`electron_orbitals`, `quantum_tunnelling`
**Library:** Kavita — reference
**Age group:** Lesson source for ages 11–12

**Concepts in Thermal Physics — Stephen J. Blundell & Katherine M. Blundell (OUP; 2nd edition 2010)**
A clean undergraduate thermodynamics text from Oxford. Covers temperature, work, heat,
entropy, the laws of thermodynamics, statistical mechanics, and the thermodynamics of
radiation. The treatment of entropy and the second law is particularly clear — essential
for explaining why things happen in one direction.
*Use for:* `thermodynamics`, `entropy`, `heat_engines`, `temperature_and_energy`, `energy_transfer`
**Library:** Kavita — reference
**Age group:** Lesson source for ages 10–12

**AQA Physics A Level — Nick England, Jeremy Pollard, Nicky Thomas & Carol Davenport (Hodder Education; 2nd edition 2018)**
The standard endorsed A-level physics textbook for the most common UK exam specification.
Directly curriculum-mapped to KS5; covers all major topic areas with clear explanations
and exam-relevant worked examples. Useful as a baseline calibrated to the vocabulary and

*Use for:* Curriculum alignment for all physics topics from GCSE to A-level.
**Library:** Kavita — reference
**Age group:** Lesson source for ages 11–12 (upper KS3 gateway to A-level)

## Philosophy — Reference (Kavita)

Introductions to ethics, epistemology, and political philosophy for intelligent
teenagers and upward. No age restriction on the Kavita library.

---

**Sophie's World — Jostein Gaarder (Aschehoug, 1991; trans. Paulette Moller, Farrar Straus Giroux, 1994)**
A novel in which a teenage girl receives a philosophy course through the post.
Covers Western philosophy from the pre-Socratics to Sartre. Widely used in schools.
The best narrative introduction to the history of philosophy.
*Use for:* `philosophy_introduction`, `history_of_western_philosophy`, `epistemology`,
`ethics_introduction`
**Library:** Kavita (also suitable for Calibre-Web) | **Age group:** 12+

**The Problems of Philosophy — Bertrand Russell (Williams & Norgate, 1912)**
Russell's short introduction to philosophy — what can we know, what is the external
world, what are universals? Written for a general audience; still the clearest
introduction to epistemology available.
*Use for:* `epistemology`, `philosophy_introduction`
**Library:** Kavita | **Age group:** 13+

**A Short History of Ethics — Alasdair MacIntyre (Routledge, 1966)**
Traces Western moral philosophy from the ancient Greeks to the 20th century.
Each chapter covers a major figure or tradition (Socrates, Aristotle, the Stoics,
Kant, Utilitarianism). The best survey of ethical theory for intelligent teenagers.
*Use for:* `ethics_introduction`, `history_of_ethics`, `political_philosophy`
**Library:** Kavita | **Age group:** 14+

**Justice: What's the Right Thing to Do? — Michael Sandel (Farrar Straus Giroux, 2009)**
Sandel's Harvard course on moral philosophy — five schools of thought (Benthamite
utility, Libertarianism, Rawls, Communitarian, Aristotelian) applied to real
dilemmas. Enormously readable.
*Use for:* `ethics_introduction`, `political_philosophy`, `justice_and_rights`
**Library:** Kavita | **Age group:** 13+

**Meditations — Marcus Aurelius (c. 161–180 AD; trans. Gregory Hays, Modern Library, 2002)**
Personal philosophical notebook of a Roman emperor. Stoic ethics applied to the
demands of power and mortality. Short sections — every passage is usable by the
tutor. The Hays translation is the best modern English version.
*Use for:* `stoic_philosophy`, `ethics_introduction`, `roman_britain` (historical context)
**Library:** Kavita | **Age group:** 12+

**The Prince — Niccolo Machiavelli (1532; trans. Tim Parks, Penguin, 2009)**
Machiavelli's advice to rulers on how to acquire and hold power. The source of
the debate about whether ends ever justify means. Short, direct, subversive.
The Parks translation is the most readable.
*Use for:* `political_philosophy`, `power_and_authority`, `pattern_authoritarian_rise`
**Library:** Kavita | **Age group:** 13+

**The Republic — Plato (c. 380 BC; trans. Tom Griffith, Cambridge University Press, 2000)**
Justice, the ideal state, the Allegory of the Cave, the philosopher-king.
*Note:* Books I–III and VI–VII (the Cave) are the most teachable; use selectively.
*Use for:* `plato_political_philosophy`, `epistemology`, `justice_and_rights`
**Library:** Kavita | **Age group:** 14+ (selective)

**Nicomachean Ethics — Aristotle (c. 350 BC; trans. Roger Crisp, Cambridge University Press, 2000)**
Aristotle on the good life — virtue, character, friendship, practical wisdom.
Books I and X (happiness and the good life) are the most accessible entry points.
*Use for:* `ethics_introduction`, `virtue_ethics`, `history_of_western_philosophy`
**Library:** Kavita | **Age group:** 14+ (selective)

**A Theory of Justice — John Rawls (Harvard University Press, 1971)**
The most important work of political philosophy of the 20th century. The veil of
ignorance thought experiment — design a just society without knowing your place in
it — is one of the most teachable ideas in all of philosophy. Use Part I (the veil
of ignorance) as a standalone extract; the rest is dense.
*Use for:* `political_philosophy`, `justice_and_rights`, `democracy_and_rights`
**Library:** Kavita | **Age group:** 14+ (targeted excerpts)

## Psychology — Reference (Kavita)

Developmental, cognitive, and social psychology texts for the AI tutor. No age restriction.

---

### Developmental Psychology

**Developmental Psychology: Childhood and Adolescence — David Shaffer (Cengage, multiple editions)**
The standard comprehensive A-level/introductory undergraduate textbook. Covers all
major theories (Piaget, Vygotsky, Erikson, Bowlby), research methods, and developmental
stages from infancy to adolescence.
*Use for:* `developmental_psychology`, `child_development`, `piaget_vygotsky`
**Library:** Kavita | **Age group:** Lesson source for ages 13+ (A-level text)

**The Developing Mind — Daniel Siegel (Guilford Press, 1999)**
Developmental psychologist's account of how the brain develops in response to
relationships. Covers attachment theory, trauma, memory, and emotional regulation.
The best single bridge between neuroscience and developmental psychology for
A-level/early undergraduate.
*Use for:* `developmental_psychology`, `attachment_theory`, `memory_and_cognition`
**Library:** Kavita | **Age group:** 14+

---

### Cognitive Psychology

**Thinking, Fast and Slow — Daniel Kahneman (Farrar Straus Giroux, 2011)**
Nobel laureate's summary of dual-process theory — System 1 (fast, intuitive) and
System 2 (slow, deliberate). Covers cognitive biases, heuristics, loss aversion,
and the limits of human rationality. The single best introduction to cognitive
psychology for a general audience.
*Use for:* `cognitive_psychology`, `decision_making`, `behavioural_economics`
**Library:** Kavita | **Age group:** 13+

**The Man Who Mistook His Wife for a Hat — Oliver Sacks (Duckworth, 1985)**
Case studies of neurological patients whose conditions reveal how the normal brain
constructs reality. Covers perception, memory, identity, and the social brain.
Each chapter is a self-contained session's worth of material.
*Use for:* `cognitive_psychology`, `neuroscience_introduction`, `memory_and_cognition`
**Library:** Kavita | **Age group:** 12+

**The Blank Slate — Steven Pinker (Penguin, 2002)**
Pinker's sustained argument that human nature is not infinitely malleable —
that there are universal aspects of mind shaped by evolution. Covers the politics
and science of the nature/nurture debate.
*Use for:* `nature_vs_nurture`, `cognitive_psychology`, `evolutionary_psychology`
**Library:** Kavita | **Age group:** 14+

---

### Social Psychology

**The Social Animal — Elliot Aronson (Worth, 1972; multiple editions)**
The most readable introduction to social psychology — conformity, persuasion,
attribution, prejudice, aggression, and prosocial behaviour. Covers Milgram, Asch,
and the major social psychology experiments with clarity and care.
*Use for:* `social_psychology`, `milgram_asch_obedience`, `prejudice_and_discrimination`
**Library:** Kavita | **Age group:** 13+

**Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion — Robert Cialdini (William Morrow, 1984; revised 2006)**
The six principles of persuasion — reciprocity, commitment, social proof, authority,
liking, and scarcity. Each chapter covers one principle with experiments and
real-world examples. Used in A-level psychology and sociology.
*Use for:* `social_psychology`, `advertising_persuasion`, `authority_and_obedience`
**Library:** Kavita | **Age group:** 13+

## Computer Science and Programming — Kavita Reference

Serious reference texts for grounding AI-generated lessons in algorithmic correctness
and systems thinking. The tutor quotes from these rather than hallucinating explanations.
Audience: upper KS2/KS3 (ages 10–12), and the AI system itself as grounding material.

**Introduction to Algorithms (CLRS) — Cormen, Leiserson, Rivest, Stein (MIT Press, 3rd ed. 2009)**
The standard reference for algorithms and data structures. Covers sorting, searching,
graph algorithms, dynamic programming, greedy algorithms, NP-completeness, and
complexity analysis. Chapters are modular — the tutor can pull the merge sort
explanation without needing the whole book.
*Use for:* `algorithms_ks3`, `data_structures`, `sorting_searching`, `complexity_theory`
**Library:** Kavita | **Age group:** Reference for tutor; excerpts for ages 12+

**The Art of Computer Programming — Donald Knuth (Addison-Wesley, Vols 1–4A)**
The deepest reference in existence for fundamental algorithms. Vol 1: Fundamental
Algorithms. Vol 2: Seminumerical Algorithms. Vol 3: Sorting and Searching.
Vol 4A: Combinatorial Algorithms. Knuth's historical notes on each algorithm
are unusually good for tutoring context ("Euclid discovered this in 300 BC...").
*Use for:* `algorithms_ks3`, `number_theory_computing`, `history_of_computing`
**Library:** Kavita | **Age group:** Reference for tutor; Knuth's historical notes excerptable for ages 12+

**Algorithms — Robert Sedgewick & Kevin Wayne (Addison-Wesley, 4th ed. 2011)**
Java-based implementations, clear diagrams, and well-paced exposition. More accessible
than CLRS for initial explanation of any algorithm — CLRS is better for formal proof.
*Use for:* `algorithms_ks3`, `data_structures`, `graphs_trees`
**Library:** Kavita | **Age group:** Excerpts accessible from age 13

**Computer Networks — Andrew Tanenbaum & David Wetherall (Pearson, 5th ed. 2010)**
The definitive reference for how the internet actually works. Covers the OSI model,
TCP/IP, Ethernet, Wi-Fi, DNS, HTTP, and TLS from first principles. Essential for
grounding any lesson on "how the internet works" beyond a hand-waving analogy.
*Use for:* `networking_ks3`, `internet_how_it_works`, `tcp_ip`, `dns_explained`
**Library:** Kavita | **Age group:** Reference for tutor; Chapter 1 excerptable for ages 12+

**Modern Operating Systems — Andrew Tanenbaum (Pearson, 4th ed. 2014)**
The standard reference for how operating systems work. Covers processes, threads,
memory management, file systems, I/O, and security. Tanenbaum writes with unusual
clarity for a systems text.
*Use for:* `operating_systems_ks3`, `processes_threads`, `file_systems`
**Library:** Kavita | **Age group:** Reference for tutor; selective excerpts for ages 13+

**Computer Architecture: A Quantitative Approach — Hennessy & Patterson (Morgan Kaufmann, 6th ed. 2017)**
The definitive reference for how processors work. RISC vs CISC, pipelining, caches,
memory hierarchies, parallelism. Turing Award winning authors. Chapter 1 gives a
complete mental model of how a CPU executes a program.
*Use for:* `computer_architecture`, `cpu_how_it_works`, `memory_caches`
**Library:** Kavita | **Age group:** Reference for tutor; Chapter 1 excerptable for ages 13+

**Applied Cryptography — Bruce Schneier (Wiley, 2nd ed. 1996)**
The accessible reference for cryptographic concepts and protocols. Covers symmetric
and asymmetric encryption, hash functions, digital signatures, and key exchange.
Chapter 1 ("Foundations") is the best single introduction to why cryptography matters.
*Use for:* `cryptography_ks3`, `encryption_how_it_works`, `public_key_crypto`,
`surveillance_capitalism`
**Library:** Kavita | **Age group:** Chapter 1 excerptable for ages 12+

**Structure and Interpretation of Computer Programs — Abelson, Sussman & Sussman (MIT Press, 2nd ed. 1996)**
The most intellectually honest introduction to programming ever written. Uses Scheme
to build programming from first principles — procedures, abstraction, recursion,
state, and metalinguistic abstraction (writing interpreters). Not a Python tutorial;
a treatment of computation itself.
*Use for:* `programming_concepts`, `recursion_explained`, `abstraction_ks3`
**Library:** Kavita | **Age group:** Reference for tutor; selected passages for ages 13+

## Mathematics — Kavita Reference

Genuine reference works and foundational texts — not school textbooks.

**Euclid's Elements (~300 BC) — translated by Thomas Heath (Green Lion Press, 2002)**
The most influential mathematics book in history. Thirteen books of geometry and
number theory built entirely from five postulates by pure logical deduction. Heath's
translation with commentary is the scholarly standard. The tutor can quote individual
proofs (Pythagoras, infinite primes) as examples of what a proof actually looks like.
*Use for:* `geometry_ks3`, `proof_and_reasoning`, `number_theory`,
`history_of_mathematics`
**Library:** Kavita | **Age group:** Reference for tutor; individual proofs excerptable from age 12

**A Mathematician's Apology — G.H. Hardy (Cambridge University Press, 1940)**
The most eloquent statement of why mathematics matters, written by a leading pure
mathematician. Hardy argues that mathematics is an art form, that the best mathematics
is beautiful and "useless," and that it lives or dies by aesthetic criteria.
Written when Hardy was old and felt his creative powers failing — honest, melancholy,
and inspiring.
*Use for:* `philosophy_of_mathematics`, `history_of_mathematics`, `what_is_proof`,
`careers_mathematics`
**Library:** Kavita | **Age group:** Excerpts accessible from age 13; full essay for age 14+

**What Is Mathematics? — Courant & Robbins (Oxford University Press, 1941; revised 1996)**
The most complete single-volume survey of mathematics as a discipline. Covers number
theory, geometry, topology, calculus, and mathematical logic. Written for the
intelligent non-specialist — demands real thought but not prior advanced knowledge.
*Use for:* `number_theory`, `geometry_ks3`, `calculus_intro`, `topology`,
`history_of_mathematics`
**Library:** Kavita | **Age group:** Selected chapters accessible from age 13

**Concrete Mathematics — Graham, Knuth & Patashnik (Addison-Wesley, 2nd ed. 1994)**
The reference for the mathematics used in computer science. Sums, recurrences,
integer functions, number theory, binomial coefficients, and generating functions.
Written with deliberate wit by Knuth and colleagues. The tutor needs this to explain
the mathematics behind algorithms correctly.
*Use for:* `combinatorics`, `number_theory`, `algorithms_ks3`, `discrete_mathematics`
**Library:** Kavita | **Age group:** Reference for tutor; selected problems excerptable for ages 14+

**The Princeton Companion to Mathematics — Timothy Gowers, ed. (Princeton University Press, 2008)**
The most comprehensive single-volume reference for modern mathematics. Essays by
leading mathematicians on every major area — number theory, algebra, geometry,
analysis, topology, logic, and the history and philosophy of the discipline.
Gowers's opening essay "What Is Mathematics?" alone is worth the volume.
*Use for:* All mathematics topics, `history_of_mathematics`, `philosophy_of_mathematics`
**Library:** Kavita | **Age group:** Reference for tutor; individual essays excerptable from age 13

**An Introduction to Probability Theory and Its Applications — William Feller (Wiley, Vol. 1, 3rd ed. 1968)**
The classic reference for probability from first principles. Feller builds probability
from combinatorics through random walks, Markov chains, and the central limit theorem.
Essential for grounding any lesson on chance, statistics, and randomness.
*Use for:* `probability_statistics_ks3`, `randomness_ks3`, `data_science_concepts`
**Library:** Kavita | **Age group:** Reference for tutor; Chapters 1–2 excerptable for ages 13+

**Gödel, Escher, Bach: An Eternal Golden Braid — Douglas Hofstadter (Basic Books, 1979)**
Interweaves Bach's canons, Escher's impossible drawings, and Gödel's incompleteness
theorems to explore how meaning emerges from symbol manipulation. Pulitzer Prize winner.
Not a textbook — a sustained philosophical meditation that teaches profound mathematics
through dialogue and story. The Lewis Carroll-style interludes work well read aloud.
*Use for:* `logic_and_proof`, `philosophy_of_mathematics`, `recursion_explained`,
`artificial_intelligence_ks3`, `godel_incompleteness`
**Library:** Kavita | **Age group:** Selected Dialogues accessible from age 13; full book for age 14+

## Technology History and Culture — Kavita Reference

**The Dream Machine — M. Mitchell Waldrop (Stripe Press, 2001; reissued 2018)**
The definitive biography of J.C.R. Licklider and the origins of personal computing.
Licklider imagined the personal computer and the internet before either existed —
and then funded the ARPA research that created them. The best single history of
how the intellectual foundations of the digital world were built.
*Use for:* `history_of_computing`, `internet_origins`, `arpa_history`,
`connection_telegraph_internet`
**Library:** Kavita | **Age group:** Reference for tutor; excerpts for ages 13+

**The Information — James Gleick (Pantheon Books, 2011)**
A history of information from the African drum to the bit. Covers Babbage and
Lovelace, Morse and the telegraph, Shannon's information theory, DNA as code,
and the internet. Gleick is the best science writer working in English — clear
prose, compelling arc, no simplification at the cost of accuracy.
*Use for:* `history_of_computing`, `information_theory`, `shannon_explained`,
`connection_telegraph_internet`
**Library:** Kavita | **Age group:** Excerpts accessible from age 13; full book for age 14+

**A Mind at Play: How Claude Shannon Invented the Information Age — Jimmy Soni & Rob Goodman (Simon & Schuster, 2017)**
The definitive biography of Claude Shannon, who invented information theory in 1948.
He proved that any message could be transmitted perfectly over a noisy channel if
coded correctly. He also rode a unicycle through Bell Labs while juggling. Perfect
for showing children that mathematicians can be weird and wonderful.
*Use for:* `information_theory`, `shannon_explained`, `history_of_computing`,
`binary_logic`
**Library:** Kavita | **Age group:** Excerpts from age 12; full book for age 13+

**The Innovators — Walter Isaacson (Simon & Schuster, 2014)**
A history of the digital revolution as a story of collaboration. Covers Ada Lovelace,
Charles Babbage, Alan Turing, John von Neumann, the transistor inventors, the ENIAC
programmers, Bob Noyce and the microchip, Bill Gates, and the internet pioneers.
Isaacson's thesis — that the most important innovations came from teams, not lone
geniuses — runs through every chapter.
*Use for:* `history_of_computing`, `ada_lovelace`, `alan_turing`, `internet_origins`
**Library:** Kavita | **Age group:** Excellent excerpts accessible from age 12

**Alan Turing: The Enigma — Andrew Hodges (Burnett Books, 1983; Princeton reissue 2012)**
The authoritative biography of Turing. Covers his mathematical work (computability,
the halting problem), Bletchley Park and the breaking of Enigma, the design of the
ACE computer, and the persecution that destroyed him. The definitive source for any
Turing-related curriculum topic.
*Use for:* `alan_turing`, `enigma_bletchley_park`, `computability_theory`,
`ww2_global_conflict`, `history_of_computing`
**Library:** Kavita | **Age group:** Reference for tutor; excerpts from age 12

**The Master Switch — Tim Wu (Knopf, 2010)**
A history of information empires — telephone, radio, film, television, internet.
Wu's "Cycle" thesis: every information technology begins open and decentralised,
is consolidated by monopoly, and either stays closed or is disrupted by the next
open technology. Essential context for any lesson about media and who controls
what we know.
*Use for:* `history_of_computing`, `internet_origins`, `media_literacy_ks3`,
`surveillance_capitalism`, `monopoly_and_markets`
**Library:** Kavita | **Age group:** Reference for tutor; excerpts from age 13

**Where Wizards Stay Up Late — Katie Hafner & Matthew Lyon (Simon & Schuster, 1996)**
The best account of how the internet was actually built. Covers ARPANET from the
first IMP contract in 1968 through the invention of email, TCP/IP, and the
transition to the public internet. Journalists' narrative — readable, precise,
based on interviews with the engineers.
*Use for:* `internet_origins`, `arpa_history`, `tcp_ip`, `history_of_computing`
**Library:** Kavita | **Age group:** Reference for tutor; excerpts from age 13

**The Shallows — Nicholas Carr (W.W. Norton, 2010)**
An argument that the internet is changing how we think, backed by neuroscience.
Carr draws on neuroplasticity research to argue that deep reading builds different
cognitive structures than scanning hyperlinked content. Balanced and evidenced —
a serious examination of attention, memory, and what reading does to a brain.
*Use for:* `attention_economy`, `media_literacy_ks3`, `reading_why_it_matters`,
`digital_wellbeing`
**Library:** Kavita | **Age group:** Excerpts accessible from age 13

## Comics and Graphic Novels

Comics occupy a unique position in this library: they are simultaneously the medium most likely to turn a reluctant reader into an enthusiastic one, and a visual language with its own grammar, history, and literary tradition. Kavita serves the tutor — reference works the AI draws on to build lessons, cite examples, and understand the medium deeply.

### Calibre-Web — Children's Reading Library

#### European Comics Beyond Asterix

The Franco-Belgian tradition produced the richest body of children's comics in the world. These belong in every child's library.

**The Adventures of Tintin — Hergé (Casterman, 1929–1976)**
Twenty-four albums following a young reporter and his fox terrier Snowy across every continent and eventually to the Moon. The series covers colonialism (Congo), Nazism (The Black Island), the Arab world (Land of Black Gold), Tibet, and space travel — always with meticulous research. Hergé's ligne claire style (clean outlines, flat colour, expressive faces) is the most influential visual language in European comics.
*Use for:* Geography, history of exploration, 20th-century political history; natural Socratic material for every album.
**Age suitability:** 8–12. Note: *Tintin in the Congo* (1931) contains colonial racism — treat as a historical document with discussion, not suppression.

| Album | Key topic |
|---|---|
| The Secret of the Unicorn / Red Rackham's Treasure | Piracy, naval history, archaeology |
| Destination Moon / Explorers on the Moon | Space exploration, rocketry — published before the actual Moon landing |
| The Calculus Affair | Cold War, scientific espionage |
| The Castafiore Emerald | Subverts the adventure formula — a locked-room mystery that goes nowhere |
| Tintin in Tibet | Himalayan geography, Buddhist culture, the Abominable Snowman |

**Lucky Luke — Morris & René Goscinny (Dupuis/Dargaud, 1946–)**
A lonesome cowboy who "shoots faster than his shadow" roams the American West, encountering Jesse James, Billy the Kid, the Dalton Brothers, and the Oregon Trail. Goscinny's writing (the same team as Asterix) makes every album a parody of a genuine historical episode. The best introductory history of the American West available for children.
*Use for:* American history, westward expansion, Native American relations (handled better in later albums).
**Age suitability:** 8–12

**Spirou and Fantasio — Franquin et al. (Dupuis, 1938–)**
A bellboy and his journalist friend travel the world encountering mad scientists, dictators, and exotic wildlife. André Franquin's work in the 1950s–60s is the series peak. More grounded in adventure and character than Tintin; excellent comic timing.
**Age suitability:** 8–12

**Blake and Mortimer — Edgar P. Jacobs (Dargaud, 1946–)**
A British RAF officer and his scientist friend investigate mysteries involving lost civilisations, time travel, Cold War espionage, and ancient Egypt. Jacobs spent years on each volume — the detail is extraordinary. *The Yellow M* is the best entry point; *The Mystery of the Great Pyramid* covers Egyptian archaeology in depth.
*Use for:* Archaeology, Cold War history, Egyptian history; `ancient_egypt`, `cold_war_context`.
**Age suitability:** 10–12

**Valerian and Laureline — Jean-Claude Mézières & Pierre Christin (Dargaud, 1967–2010)**
Twenty-two albums of science-fiction space opera following two spatio-temporal agents. Mézières' ship and alien designs directly inspired Star Wars. The series engages with colonialism (Point Central), ecology (On the Frontiers), and political systems (The Empire of a Thousand Planets) decades before those became standard science-fiction themes.
*Use for:* Ecology, political systems, science fiction as social commentary; `world_economies`, `governance_political_systems`.
**Age suitability:** 10–12

#### British Comics Heritage

**Beano: Greatest Moments and Classic Collections — DC Thomson (1938–)**
Dennis the Menace, Minnie the Minx, Roger the Dodger, Billy Whizz. Published continuously since 1938 — the longest-running weekly comic in the world. Essential British cultural heritage. The humour is anarchic and child-centred: children always outmanoeuvre adults.
*Use for:* British cultural capital, comic timing, visual humour.
**Age suitability:** 6–10

**Dandy Classic Comics — DC Thomson (1937–)**
Desperate Dan and Korky the Cat. The Dandy predates the Beano by one year. The physical comedy (Dan's iron jaw, his cow pies) is quintessentially British slapstick.
**Age suitability:** 6–10

**Dan Dare: Pilot of the Future — Frank Hampson (Eagle Comics, 1950–1969)**
Britain's greatest science-fiction comic strip, running for nineteen years in *Eagle* magazine. Squadron Leader Dan Dare protects Earth from the Mekon's Treens. Hampson's artwork was painted with reference models and costumes — technically extraordinary for the period. The series reflects 1950s British optimism about science and empire.
*Why it works:* The values (service, courage, fair play) and the science (rocketry, alien life, space physics) both invite discussion. The Mekon as rational-but-amoral intelligence is a genuine philosophical figure.
*Use for:* Space exploration history, British cultural history, ethics of intelligence vs. wisdom; `space_exploration_ks2`, `cold_war_context`.
**Age suitability:** 9–12

*Note: Eagle Annual collections (Hulton Press, 1950s–60s) are worth acquiring alongside Dan Dare — they mixed comics with factual articles on science and history, a direct ancestor of the etutor concept.*

#### Literary Comics for Ages 9–12

**Nausicaä of the Valley of the Wind — Hayao Miyazaki (Tokuma Shoten, 1982–1994)**
Seven volumes of post-apocalyptic manga. One thousand years after an industrial civilisation destroyed itself, a young princess tries to prevent a second catastrophe by understanding the toxic jungle rather than fighting it. Written and drawn entirely by Miyazaki over twelve years, this is the source work for the Studio Ghibli film — and the manga goes far beyond the film in depth and moral complexity.
*Why it works:* Nausicaä is the curriculum's ecology and environmental science section made flesh — a child who studies insects, understands ecological systems, and refuses to accept the adults' binary of destroy-or-be-destroyed. The ethical framework (understanding vs. domination; short-term safety vs. long-term survival) maps directly to climate change discussions.
*Use for:* Ecology, environmental science, ethics of science, anti-war themes; `environmental_protection_ks2`, `ecosystems_food_chains`.
**Age suitability:** 10–12 (some violence and death; handled with gravity not spectacle)

**March (Trilogy) — John Lewis, Andrew Aydin; art Nate Powell (Top Shelf, 2013–2016)**
Congressman John Lewis's memoir of the American civil rights movement, told in black-and-white comics form. Three volumes from childhood on an Alabama farm through the March on Washington, Bloody Sunday at Selma, and the passage of the Voting Rights Act. Powell's art is extraordinary — the movement of crowds, the violence of the police, the silence of the church bomb.
*Why it works:* Primary-source autobiography of the civil rights movement by a participant, in comics form. Children understand it before they understand *To Kill a Mockingbird* because Lewis is telling his own story. The non-violent direct action methods are explained from the inside.
*Use for:* Civil rights, American history, non-violent resistance; `civil_rights_global`, `civil_disobedience_theory`.
**Age suitability:** 10–12 for Vol. 1; 11–12 for Vols. 2–3 (increasing violence)
**Also:** Acquire for Kavita as a primary lesson-source text.

**Classics Illustrated — Various (Gilberton Company / Papercutz, 1941–)**
The original run (1941–1971) adapted 169 works of classic literature into comics form. Papercutz relaunched the series in 2007. Essential titles: *Moby Dick*, *Great Expectations*, *Les Misérables*, *The Three Musketeers*, *The Hunchback of Notre Dame*, *20,000 Leagues Under the Sea*, *Hamlet*, *Romeo and Juliet*, *A Midsummer Night's Dream*.
*Why it works:* Follows the two-pass strategy naturally — comics version first, original text after. Children who have read the Classics Illustrated *Hamlet* approach the original Shakespeare with a plot they already know.
*Use for:* Every text in the Shakespeare and older literature sections of this file.
**Age suitability:** 9–12

**The Odyssey — Gareth Hinds (Candlewick Press, 2010)**
Full adaptation of Homer's Odyssey in 248 pages of watercolour comics. Better than any of the Classics Illustrated versions for this text. Hinds renders the Greek mythological world with genuine visual weight — the Cyclops is terrifying, the sirens are beautiful and dangerous, Ithaca is worth fighting for.
*Use for:* Greek mythology, ancient history, epic narrative structure; `ancient_greece`, `art_history_ancient_renaissance`.
**Age suitability:** 9–12

*Also in the Hinds series: Beowulf (2007), King Lear (2009), The Merchant of Venice (2008) — all recommended.*

#### Alan Moore — Complete Works Beyond Watchmen

Watchmen is already acquired. The rest of Moore's major work forms a coherent body of criticism of power, narrative, and the superhero genre.

**V for Vendetta — Alan Moore & David Lloyd (DC/Vertigo, 1988–89)**
In a fascist Britain that has survived nuclear war, a masked anarchist wages a one-person war against the state. Drawn in grey-and-black with occasional colour for emphasis.
*Why it matters for the tutor:* The most rigorous fiction about the tension between order and freedom. The novel complicates V's moral position — is he a terrorist? a teacher? a father? — in ways that map directly to the dissent and authority curriculum. It predates and inspired the Guy Fawkes mask's adoption as a political symbol; knowing the source is essential for understanding the icon.
*Use for:* `pattern_authoritarian_rise`, `civil_disobedience_theory`, `censorship_history`, `surveillance_capitalism`.
**Age suitability:** 14+

**From Hell — Alan Moore & Eddie Campbell (Top Shelf, 1999)**
A 572-page account of the Jack the Ripper murders focused on the Victorian city of London. Exhaustively researched — each chapter has an appendix explaining sources. Not a whodunit; a study of how Victorian class, gender, and medicine intersected in the East End. Chapter 2 (the carriage tour of London) is a forty-page history of London architecture that functions as a standalone essay.
*Use for:* Victorian social history, London geography, medical history, class and gender in the 19th century; `victorian_britain`, `industrial_revolution`.
**Age suitability:** 16+ (graphic violence, sexual content)

**Top 10 — Alan Moore & Gene Ha (America's Best Comics, 1999–2001)**
A police procedural set in Neopolis, a city where every resident has superpowers. A superhero premise that enables Moore to explore every police drama trope (racism, domestic violence, corruption, class prejudice) while the costumes simultaneously comment on how genre works.
*Use for:* Police procedure, social justice themes, media literacy (genre as ideology).
**Age suitability:** 16+

**Promethea — Alan Moore & J.H. Williams III (America's Best Comics, 1999–2005)**
A college student becomes the latest avatar of Promethea, a being of pure imagination. Over 32 issues, Moore delivers an encyclopaedia of Western mysticism, the history of magic and art, and the Kabbalistic Tree of Life. Williams III's layout experiments — issues drawn as tarot cards, as a Möbius strip, as a double-page spread that reads in eight different directions — are the most formally adventurous in mainstream comics.
*Use for:* Art history, Western mythology, the history of magic and religion; `art_appreciation_visual_literacy`, `world_religions_comparison`.
**Age suitability:** 16+

#### Frank Miller

**The Dark Knight Returns — Frank Miller, Klaus Janson, Lynn Varley (DC Comics, 1986)**
A fifty-five-year-old Bruce Wayne comes out of retirement in a Reaganite America that has outlawed superheroes. Four issues that redefined superhero comics as a literary form and introduced the genre to political satire. The Superman fight in Issue 4 — the state versus the individual — is one of the most debated sequences in comics.
*Note: Batman is already in Kavita. This specific work is distinct and essential as a literary reference point for the history of the medium.*

Seven interconnected noir crime stories set in Basin City. *Use for:* Film noir, visual contrast in art, American crime fiction; `art_composition_perspective`.

**300 — Frank Miller & Lynn Varley (Dark Horse, 1998)**
The Battle of Thermopylae as heroic myth. Miller's interpretation foregrounds Spartan propaganda — the 300 are shown through Spartan eyes, which is the point. Invites discussion of how societies construct military mythology, and how the same events look from the Persian perspective.
*Use for:* Ancient Greek history, Spartan society, propaganda and historical memory; `ancient_greece`.

#### History Through Comics

**The Complete Maus: A Survivor's Tale — Art Spiegelman (Pantheon, 1986 / 1991)**
*(Expanded from the one-line mention in the Art section above.)*
Two volumes. Vladek Spiegelman's account of surviving Auschwitz, told to his son Art, who draws the story using animal metaphor: Jews as mice, Nazis as cats, Poles as pigs, Americans as dogs. The framing device — Art interviewing his difficult, elderly father in the present — is as important as the Holocaust narrative. The second volume interrogates the ethics of the metaphor itself.
*Why it works for the tutor:* Maus is simultaneously a Holocaust memoir, a son-father story, and a meditation on how to represent atrocity. The animal metaphor generates Socratic questions naturally: "Why did Spiegelman draw Jews as mice — what does it say about how the Nazis saw them?"
*Use for:* `ww2_global_conflict`, `pattern_authoritarian_rise`, historiography, narrative ethics.
**Age suitability:** 12+ (some graphic content; the animal metaphor mediates the horror)
**Library:** Both Calibre-Web (ages 12+) and Kavita (as primary reference)

**Persepolis — Marjane Satrapi (L'Association/Pantheon, 2000–2003)**
*(Expanded from the one-line mention in the Art section above.)*
Satrapi's memoir of growing up in Tehran during and after the Islamic Revolution of 1979. Two volumes, combined as one in the English edition. Black-and-white, deceptively simple figures. The child's-eye view of revolution — adults acting incomprehensibly, institutions changing overnight, friends disappearing — is the most accessible account of how authoritarian change feels from inside.
*Use for:* `authoritarianism_types`, `censorship_history`, Islamic Revolution, women's rights; a companion text to *1984* for the surveillance and authority topics.
**Age suitability:** 12+ (Calibre-Web); all ages as reference in Kavita

**Palestine — Joe Sacco (Fantagraphics, 1993–2001)**
Nine issues of journalism comics, later collected as a single volume. Sacco spent two months in the West Bank and Gaza in 1991–92 interviewing Palestinians under occupation. The cartooning style — dense crosshatching, crowded panels, the author himself always visible — announces itself as subjective reportage, not objective truth-claim.
*Why it matters:* Sacco invented a new form: the journalist who puts their own limitations on the page. *Palestine* is both a political document and a lesson in epistemology — what it means to witness, to translate, to represent.
*Use for:* Middle East history, journalism ethics, visual journalism; `censorship_history`, `media_literacy_ks3`.
**Age suitability:** 14+

**Footnotes in Gaza — Joe Sacco (Metropolitan Books, 2009)**
Sacco returns to Gaza to investigate two 1956 massacres — events almost entirely absent from the historical record. The research process (why does memory fade? what counts as evidence?) is woven into the narrative. A masterclass in investigative journalism and historical methodology.
*Use for:* Historical methodology, memory and evidence, journalism ethics.
**Age suitability:** 15+

**Logicomix: An Epic Search for Truth — Apostolos Doxiadis & Christos Papadimitriou; art Alecos Papadatos & Annie Di Donna (Bloomsbury, 2009)**
Bertrand Russell narrates the quest for the logical foundations of mathematics — from Frege's *Grundgesetze* through Whitehead and Russell's *Principia Mathematica* to Gödel's incompleteness theorems. A drama about the relationship between logical certainty and madness. Russell himself is a character — brilliant, emotionally constrained, morally serious in a Victorian way.
*Why it matters:* The only comics treatment of formal logic and the foundations of mathematics written at a level intelligent teenagers can approach.

*Use for:* `ww1_causes_consequences`, anti-war literature, French history.

*(Expanded from the one-line mention in the Art section above.)*

*Why it matters for the tutor:* Demonstrates how much the medium can do when freed from genre conventions. Useful for the visual literacy sections: Ware explicitly engages with architectural history and American industrial design.

## Illustrated Picture Books — Calibre-Web (Ages 5–8)

Illustrated picture books with serious literary or artistic merit. Selected for
value as read-along texts and for teaching visual literacy alongside verbal literacy.

**Where the Wild Things Are — Maurice Sendak (Harper & Row, 1963)**
Max is sent to bed without supper and imagines a land of wild things where he is
king — then chooses to come home.
*Why it works:* The illustrations get larger as the fantasy grows and smaller as
it retreats — pure visual storytelling. The best picture book for teaching the
relationship between words and pictures.
*Questions:* "Why does Max want to go back home even though he is king?" / "How
do the pictures change when the wild rumpus starts?"
**Library:** Calibre-Web | **Age group:** 5–8 (Y1–3)
**Use for:** `art_appreciation_visual_literacy`

**The Snowman — Raymond Briggs (Hamish Hamilton, 1978)**
Entirely wordless. A boy builds a snowman who comes to life; they spend a night
of adventures together. The snowman melts by morning.
*Why it works:* Teaches visual narrative sequencing without words. The ending
(grief without explanation) is the first serious loss many small children
encounter in a book.
*Questions:* "How did you know what was happening even without words?"
/ "How did the ending make you feel — and why?"
**Library:** Calibre-Web | **Age group:** 5–8 (Y1–3)
**Use for:** `art_appreciation_visual_literacy`

**Gorilla — Anthony Browne (Julia MacRae Books, 1983)**
Hannah loves gorillas but her father is too busy to take her to the zoo. A toy
gorilla transforms overnight into a real gorilla who takes her on the trip she
has always wanted.
*Why it works:* Browne's illustrations are packed with visual jokes and hidden
references.
*Questions:* "Is the gorilla real or a dream — does it matter?" / "What do you
notice in the pictures if you look really carefully?"
**Library:** Calibre-Web | **Age group:** 5–8 (Y1–3)
**Use for:** `art_appreciation_visual_literacy`

**Mog the Forgetful Cat — Judith Kerr (Collins, 1970)**
Mog is a family cat who keeps forgetting things — then accidentally saves the
family. By the same author as The Tiger Who Came to Tea.
*Questions:* "Is it Mog's fault that she forgets things?" / "How does Mog feel
when the family is cross with her?"
**Library:** Calibre-Web | **Age group:** 5–7 (Y1–2)

**The Enormous Crocodile — Roald Dahl, illus. Quentin Blake (Jonathan Cape, 1978)**
The Enormous Crocodile has "secret plans and clever tricks" to eat children. The
other jungle animals defeat him.
*Why it works:* Dahl's briefest picture book and his most purely funny. Good
entry point to Dahl before the chapter books.
*Questions:* "Why do all the other animals want to stop the crocodile?" / "What
makes the crocodile's disguises not very good?"
**Library:** Calibre-Web | **Age group:** 5–7 (Y1–2)

## Music Books for Children — Calibre-Web

**The Story of Music — Howard Goodall (Chatto & Windus / BBC, 2013)**
Companion to the BBC TV series. Tells the history of Western music in narrative
form — why music sounds different in different eras, how melody, rhythm, and
harmony developed.
*Why it works:* Short, self-contained chapters. Each covers one musical innovation.
The tutor can use chapters as the basis for listening sessions.
*Questions:* "Why did people decide they needed more than one note at a time?"
/ "What changed when music could be written down?"
**Library:** Calibre-Web | **Age group:** 10–13 (Y5–8)
**Use for:** Music history curriculum

**Beethoven Lives Upstairs — Barbara Nichol (Scholastic, 1993)**
A chapter book told through letters between a boy and his uncle. Beethoven has
moved in upstairs and the boy finds him disturbing — until he begins to understand
what the music means. Warm introduction to Beethoven's life and deafness.
**Library:** Calibre-Web | **Age group:** 8–11 (Y3–6)
**Use for:** Music history curriculum, biography

**Peter and the Wolf — Sergei Prokofiev (1936) — illustrated edition**
Prokofiev's orchestral tale assigns each character a different instrument — Peter
is the strings, the wolf is the horns, the bird is the flute. The definitive
introduction to the orchestra for children. The Jörg Müller illustrated edition
(North-South Books) is the most visually striking.
*Why it works:* Children learn to recognise instrumental timbres by associating
them with characters.
*Questions:* "Why did Prokofiev give each character their own instrument?"
/ "Which instrument sounds most like its character and why?"
**Library:** Calibre-Web | **Age group:** 6–10 (Y1–5)
**Use for:** Music curriculum — instruments and families

**The Usborne Story of Music — Megan Cullis (Usborne, 2018)**
A narrative introduction to classical music for ages 8–12. Lighter than Goodall;
good for younger children before progressing to The Story of Music.
**Library:** Calibre-Web | **Age group:** 8–11 (Y3–6)
**Use for:** Music history curriculum

## Mythology and Folklore — Calibre-Web and Kavita

**The Iliad — Homer, retold by Roger Lancelyn Green (Penguin Classics, 1956)**
Green's retellings of Greek epic remain the best prose versions for children —
faithful to the plot and spirit, written in clear, vivid English.
*Questions:* "Is Achilles right to stop fighting?" / "Who is more heroic —
Achilles or Hector?"
**Library:** Calibre-Web | **Age group:** 9–12 (Y4–7)
**Use for:** `ancient_greece`, `art_history_ancient_renaissance`

**The Odyssey — Homer, retold by Roger Lancelyn Green (Penguin Classics, 1953)**
The more episodic and accessible of the two epics — each adventure (the Cyclops,
the Sirens, Circe, the Underworld) is self-contained.
*Questions:* "Why does Odysseus tell the Cyclops his name — and why is that a
mistake?" / "What does it mean to be clever rather than strong?"
**Library:** Calibre-Web | **Age group:** 9–12 (Y4–7)
**Use for:** `ancient_greece`

**Tales of the Greek Heroes — Roger Lancelyn Green (Penguin, 1958)**
Covers Heracles, Perseus, Jason and the Argonauts, Theseus, and the other major
Greek hero myths. The standard Calibre-Web companion to the Iliad and Odyssey.
**Library:** Calibre-Web | **Age group:** 8–11 (Y3–6)
**Use for:** `ancient_greece`

**Norse Myths: Tales of Odin, Thor and Loki — Kevin Crossley-Holland (Walker Books, 2017)**
Crossley-Holland's retellings are the gold standard for Norse mythology — he is
both a poet and a scholar of Old English. This illustrated edition covers the full
cycle from the creation to Ragnarok in accessible, beautifully written prose.
*Why it works:* Connects directly to `vikings_saxons` and to Beowulf. Children
already know Loki and Thor from popular culture — this gives them the real source.
*Questions:* "How is the Norse idea of the end of the world different from what
you expected?" / "Why does Odin keep seeking wisdom even though it costs him
so much?"
**Library:** Calibre-Web | **Age group:** 9–12 (Y4–7)
**Use for:** `vikings_saxons`

**Myths of the Norsemen — Roger Lancelyn Green (Puffin, 1960)**
An earlier, simpler retelling than Crossley-Holland — useful as a first encounter
for ages 8–10.
**Library:** Calibre-Web | **Age group:** 8–10 (Y3–5)
**Use for:** `vikings_saxons`

**In the Beginning: Creation Myths from Around the World — Virginia Hamilton (Harcourt, 1988)**
Twenty-five creation myths from different cultures, illustrated by Barry Moser.
Includes Mayan, Babylonian, Norse, Aboriginal Australian, and West African versions.
*Why it works:* Comparative mythology is a natural entry into world religions and
the question of why all cultures have these stories. Places the King James Bible's
creation narrative alongside others.
*Questions:* "What do all these creation stories have in common?" / "Why do you
think every culture has a story about how the world began?"
**Library:** Calibre-Web | **Age group:** 9–12 (Y4–7)
**Use for:** World religions curriculum, comparative mythology

**Folk Tales of England — James Reeves (Faber, 1954)**
English folk tales — Jack and the Beanstalk, Tom Tit Tot, The Three Sillies —
told in literary prose that respects the oral tradition. Indigenous English stories,
distinct from fairy tales imported from Europe.
**Library:** Calibre-Web | **Age group:** 7–10 (Y2–5)
**Use for:** `reading_comprehension_ks2`, oral tradition

**The Norse Myths — Kevin Crossley-Holland (Pantheon, 1980)**
The full scholarly adult version with introduction and notes. Goes in Kavita as
the reference edition alongside the children's Walker Books version above.
**Library:** Kavita | **Age group:** Reference — tutor use; excerpts 11+
**Use for:** `vikings_saxons`

**World Mythology — Roy Willis, editor (Duncan Baird, 1993; revised 2006)**
Covers the myths of every major world culture — Egyptian, Mesopotamian, Indian,
Chinese, Japanese, Norse, Celtic, African, American, and Oceanic. The tutor's
primary lookup when a child asks about mythology outside the Greek and Norse
traditions.
**Library:** Kavita | **Age group:** Reference — tutor use; excerpts 10+
**Use for:** World religions curriculum, `ancient_egypt`, comparative mythology

**Celtic Myths and Legends — T.W. Rolleston (George Harrap, 1917; Dover reprint)**
The standard reference for Irish and Welsh mythology — the Mabinogion, the Ulster
Cycle, Cú Chulainn, Finn McCool. Tutor reference rather than child-facing.
**Library:** Kavita | **Age group:** Reference — tutor use; excerpts 12+
**Use for:** Celtic history, British Isles history

## How to Use in etutor-server

1. **Add to Calibre-Web** — upload EPUBs or PDFs; set metadata tags matching
   curriculum topic IDs so the recommender can surface them.

2. **Read-along passages** — extract age-appropriate excerpts (100–200 words)
   from narrative histories (TSI, Horrible Histories) for the read-along session
   mode. The child reads while Piper TTS narrates; AI asks comprehension questions
   about the passage.

3. **Session context enrichment** — when a child is studying a history topic,
   inject a 2-3 sentence narrative hook from TSI into the system prompt:
   "Today you're exploring the Great Fire of London. In 1666, a small fire in
   a baker's shop on Pudding Lane spread through the wooden houses of medieval
   London..." — gives the tutor a shared story to reference.

4. **Book recommendations** — when the interest graph detects "vikings",
   "egypt", or "world war", surface the matching DK Eyewitness or Horrible
   Histories title as a next-session suggestion.
